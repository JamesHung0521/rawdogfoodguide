Dachshunds, those beloved "sausage dogs" with their long bodies and short legs, face a health crisis that no owner should ignore. Intervertebral Disc Disease (IVDD) affects roughly 25% of Dachshunds during their lives, often requiring expensive surgery or leaving dogs paralyzed. The right nutrition—from raw feeding—can significantly reduce this risk.

Dachshund Nutritional Needs

Dachshunds come in two sizes: standard (16-32 pounds) and miniature (under 11 pounds). Both sizes share the same elongated body structure that puts them at risk for back problems. Despite their short legs, Dachshunds are active, athletic dogs originally bred to hunt badgers—digging into burrows with tenacity.

Proper nutrition supports their active lifestyle while maintaining the ideal body weight that protects their spine. Too much weight stresses the long back and increases IVDD risk. Too little food, especially in puppies, can lead to developmental problems. Balance is key.

The breed's body structure—roughly eight times longer than tall—means their spine bears different stresses than a square-proportioned dog. The discs between vertebrae experience more compression and shearing forces. Supporting spine health through nutrition becomes especially important.

Dachshunds tend to gain weight easily, which is particularly dangerous for their body type. Those extra pounds multiply stress on the spine with every movement. Many overweight Dachshunds develop back problems years earlier than they would at healthy weight.

The Back Health Crisis

Intervertebral Disc Disease is the most significant health concern for Dachshunds. The breed's genetic history selected for a chondrodystrophic body type—short legs and long spine—which unfortunately comes with weakened spinal discs that degenerate earlier than in other breeds.

Dogs with IVDD experience disc herniation when the cushioning discs between vertebrae bulge or rupture. This presses on the spinal cord, causing pain, weakness, and in severe cases, paralysis. Surgery can cost $5,000-15,000, and not all dogs recover fully.

Nutrition can't eliminate genetic predisposition, but it can significantly influence disc health and overall risk. Several dietary factors play important roles:

Collagen and connective tissue support comes from adequate protein and specific nutrients. The discs themselves are made of collagen and require amino acids like glycine, proline, and lysine to maintain their structure. Raw meaty bones provide these building blocks naturally as your dog consumes cartilage and connective tissue.

Anti-inflammatory nutrition reduces wear and tear on spinal structures. Omega-3 fatty acids from raw fish decrease inflammation throughout the body, including in the spine. Many Dachshunds with early-stage disc issues benefit from increased omega-3 intake.

Weight control is perhaps the most critical factor. Every extra pound puts multiplied stress on a Dachshund's long spine. Maintaining lean body weight throughout life significantly reduces IVDD risk and severity.

Calcium and phosphorus balance ensures strong vertebrae. Raw bones provide calcium and phosphorus in the ideal ratios for bone health. The cartilage and connective tissue in raw meaty bones also supports disc health.

Weight Control for Spine Safety

If you do nothing else for your Dachshund's back health, control their weight. This single factor may be the most important determinant of whether your dog develops IVDD.

Dachshunds should be lean, not fat. You should be able to feel (but not see) their ribs when running your hands along their sides. There should be a visible waist when viewed from above, and the belly should tuck up behind the ribcage when viewed from the side. No sagging belly or fat rolls.

It's easy to overfeed Dachshunds because they look cute with a little extra weight—and because they excel at begging. That soft, soulful expression is a survival mechanism developed over millennia. Resist the temptation. Your Dachshund's spine will thank you.

Raw feeding helps with weight management because protein and fat satisfy more than carbohydrates. High-protein diets preserve lean muscle while reducing fat storage. Many owners find their Dachshunds become calmer and less food-focused after switching to raw, which makes portion control easier.

Weigh your Dachshund monthly on a scale. Even small weight gains add up over time. A half-pound on a 20-pound dog is 2.5% of body weight; on a 10-pound miniature, it's 5%—significant stress on the spine. Catch weight gain early and adjust portions before it becomes a problem.

Joint Support Nutrition

Dachshunds are prone to joint problems beyond disc disease. Hip dysplasia, luxating patellas (especially in miniatures), and osteoarthritis all affect the breed. Supporting joint health through nutrition provides benefits throughout life.

Glucosamine and chondroitin build and maintain cartilage. Raw meaty bones provide these compounds naturally when your dog consumes cartilage and connective tissue. Chicken feet, duck feet, and turkey necks are excellent sources. If your Dachshund has difficulty with whole bones, green-lipped mussel powder provides supplemental glucosamine and chondroitin.

Collagen peptides support joint flexibility and connective tissue strength. Bone broth is an excellent source of collagen—simmer raw bones for 24-48 hours to extract the collagen. Add a splash to meals for joint support.

Omega-3 fatty acids reduce joint inflammation. Joint stiffness decreases as inflammation decreases. Feed raw fish like sardines or mackerel 2-3 times per week, or add fish oil to meals.

Vitamin C supports collagen synthesis. While dogs produce their own vitamin C, additional amounts from foods like organ meats may benefit connective tissue health. This is especially relevant for senior Dachshunds.

Daily Portion Guidelines

Dachshunds typically need 2-2.5% of body weight in raw food daily for weight maintenance. Less active or senior dogs might need 1.5-2%, while younger, active dogs might need 2.5-3%.

Here's a reference for typical adult portions:

  • Miniature (8 lb): 2.5-3.2 oz daily
  • Miniature (10 lb): 3.2-4 oz daily
  • Standard (18 lb): 5.8-7.2 oz daily
  • Standard (25 lb): 8-10 oz daily

These are starting points. Adjust based on your individual dog's body condition. If your Dachshund is gaining weight, reduce portions. If ribs are becoming prominent, increase slightly. The goal is lean and muscular, not thin and bony.

Dachshund puppies need more food relative to size—typically 5-8% of body weight daily, split across three meals. Growing puppies need substantial nutrition for bone and muscle development. Never severely restrict puppy food. However, avoid overfeeding—rapid growth can stress developing bones.

Senior Dachshunds often need portion adjustments. Less active older dogs burn fewer calories. Watch weight carefully and reduce portions if needed. Some seniors benefit from increased protein to prevent muscle loss. Check out our portion calculator for personalized recommendations.

Best Foods for Dachshunds

Dachshunds do well on variety of proteins. Rotation feeding ensures comprehensive nutrition and prevents sensitivity development.

Chicken is an excellent foundation protein. Dark meat provides extra fat for energy. Chicken backs, necks, and wings all work—backs are particularly good value and provide variety in bone content.

Turkey offers similar benefits with different amino acid profiles. Ground turkey works well for Dachshunds who prefer softer textures.

Duck is a good rotation protein and provides omega-3s. Duck feet are excellent for joint health and dental cleaning. Duck necks provide meaty bone content.

Beef provides iron and zinc. Ground beef with visible fat works well. Beef trachea provides cartilage for joint health.

Fish provides essential omega-3 fatty acids for spine and joint health. Sardines, mackerel, and salmon are all good options. Feed fish 2-3 times per week.

Rabbit is lean and provides variety. Some Dachshunds with chicken sensitivities do well on rabbit.

Organ meats should comprise about 10% of diet, with half being liver. Variety in organ meats provides comprehensive nutrition.

Back-Safe Feeding Practices

How you feed matters for Dachshunds. Certain practices reduce back stress and injury risk.

Use elevated feeding stations. Raised bowls reduce neck and back strain during eating. Dachshunds who eat from floor-level bowls spend mealtimes in a head-down position that stresses their spine. Elevated feeders promote neutral spine alignment.

Prevent jumping. Discourage Dachshunds from jumping on and off furniture. Consider ramps for couch and bed access. Jumping creates compressive forces on the spine that increase disc herniation risk. Some owners use baby gates to limit access to elevated furniture.

Support the back. When picking up your Dachshund, support both the chest and rear end. Never lift by the front legs only or by the scruff. This prevents hyperextension of the spine.

Encourage swimming. If your Dachshund enjoys water, swimming provides excellent exercise with minimal spine stress. The buoyancy supports the body while allowing muscle building and cardiovascular conditioning.

Maintain muscle tone. Strong core and back muscles support the spine. Regular, moderate exercise helps maintain the muscular corset that protects vertebrae and discs. Avoid extreme exercise that jars the spine, but keep your Dachshund active and muscular.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can raw feeding prevent IVDD in Dachshunds?

Raw feeding can't eliminate genetic predisposition, but it significantly reduces IVDD risk and severity. Proper nutrition maintains healthy disc tissue, supports connective tissue strength, provides anti-inflammatory compounds, and most importantly, helps control weight. Many owners of IVDD-prone Dachshunds credit raw feeding with keeping their dogs symptom-free well past the age when problems typically appear.

What supplements help prevent back problems?

Green-lipped mussel provides glucosamine and chondroitin for disc and joint health. Fish oil provides omega-3s that reduce inflammation. Collagen peptides support connective tissue. Vitamin D supports calcium metabolism for bone health. Discuss specific supplements with your vet, especially if your Dachshund shows any back pain symptoms.

How much should I feed my Dachshund to prevent obesity?

Start with 2-2.5% of body weight for adults. Miniatures typically need 2.5-4 oz daily; standards typically need 6-10 oz daily. Adjust based on body condition—you should feel but not see ribs. Weigh monthly and reduce portions if gaining weight. Even small daily overages add up significantly over time.

Can Dachshunds eat bones safely?

Yes, raw meaty bones are safe and beneficial for most Dachshunds. The vertebrae that protect the spine also need exercise, and chewing provides that. Choose appropriately sized bones—chicken backs, necks, and wings work for most Dachshunds. Never feed cooked bones. If your Dachshund has existing back problems, discuss bone feeding with your vet.

Should I feed my Dachshund puppy differently for bone development?

Dachshund puppies need 5-8% of body weight daily for proper growth. Focus on providing adequate calcium and phosphorus for developing bones—raw meaty bones provide these in ideal ratios. Avoid excessive calories that cause rapid growth, which can stress developing vertebrae. Growing puppies should eat three meals daily. Discuss growth rates with your vet to ensure your puppy is developing at an appropriate pace.

What are signs of back pain in Dachshunds?

Watch for reluctance to jump or climb stairs, hunching the back, crying when picked up or touched along the spine, weakness in back legs, uncoordinated gait, and loss of appetite due to pain. If you notice any of these signs, contact your vet immediately. Early intervention improves outcomes significantly.

How does raw feeding compare to commercial food for Dachshunds?

Commercial foods often contain inflammatory ingredients, excessive carbohydrates that promote weight gain, and processed proteins that may not support tissue health as well as fresh whole foods. Raw feeding provides bioavailable nutrients, supports healthy weight, reduces inflammation, and supplies the building blocks for strong discs and connective tissue. Many Dachshund owners report fewer back problems and better overall health after switching to raw.
