Shih Tzus were bred to be companion dogs for Chinese royalty. Those soulful eyes and luxurious coats require dedicated care, and nutrition plays a huge role in keeping your "Little Lion" looking and feeling their best. Raw feeding addresses many common Shih Tzu health issues that plague the breed.

Understanding Shih Tzu Nutrition

Adult Shih Tzus typically weigh between 9 and 16 pounds, with healthy adults often falling in the 10-12 pound range. They have compact, sturdy builds despite their small size, with broad heads, shortened muzzles, and long, flowing coats.

The breed's name translates to "Lion Dog" in Chinese, referencing their appearance and historical role as lap dogs for emperors. They were bred primarily for companionship rather than work, which means they tend to be less active than terriers or sporting breeds. This lower activity level means they burn fewer calories and are prone to weight gain if overfed.

Shih Tzus have brachycephalic (flat) faces, which affects breathing and makes them prone to overheating. Their shortened airways mean they can't cool themselves efficiently through panting. This isn't usually a major dietary concern, but it's worth keeping in mind during exercise and hot weather—strenuous activity and heavy meals don't mix well for flat-faced dogs.

The breed has a reputation for being somewhat food-motivated, which actually makes them good candidates for raw feeding. Most Shih Tzus transition readily to raw once they develop a taste for meat. Their enthusiasm for food can lead to overfeeding, though, so portion control remains important.

Skin & Coat Health

The Shih Tzu's beautiful coat is their crowning glory—and often their biggest grooming challenge. The breed's long, double coat requires constant attention, and many Shih Tzus struggle with skin problems that manifest as itching, redness, hot spots, and dull coat quality.

Most commercial dog foods contain ingredients that trigger skin inflammation in sensitive dogs. Grains, soy, artificial preservatives, and rendered proteins can all cause or worsen skin issues. Raw feeding eliminates these problem ingredients, replacing them with whole, minimally processed foods that are easier on the immune system.

Omega-3 fatty acids are essential for Shih Tzu coat health. Found primarily in fish and fish oils, EPA and DHA reduce skin inflammation and support follicle health. Many Shih Tzus with chronic itching show significant improvement with added omega-3s. Feed raw fish like sardines or mackerel 2-3 times per week, or add a small amount of fish oil to meals.

Protein quality directly affects coat condition. Hair is made of keratin, an animal protein, and requires adequate amino acids to grow strong and shiny. Lower-quality proteins can't support optimal coat growth. Raw meat provides complete amino acid profiles in highly digestible forms.

Zinc from organ meats supports skin cell turnover and healing. Many Shih Tzus with chronic skin problems benefit from increased zinc intake. Liver, kidney, and heart all provide zinc along with other essential nutrients.

Vitamin A from moderate liver consumption supports skin health. However, too much vitamin A causes toxicity, so stick to the 5% liver rule—liver should comprise about 5% of total food intake, not the majority.

Managing Allergies

Shih Tzus are prone to food allergies and sensitivities. The breed's popularity has led to extensive breeding that sometimes prioritizes appearance over health, resulting in immune systems that react poorly to common ingredients.

Common allergens for Shih Tzus include chicken, beef, dairy, wheat, corn, and soy. These ingredients dominate commercial dog foods, which explains why so many Shih Tzus suffer from chronic ear infections, skin itching, and digestive upset on kibble diets.

The challenge is figuring out which ingredient triggers your individual dog. Elimination diets offer the most reliable way to identify food allergies. Choose a novel protein that your Shih Tzu has never eaten—lamb, venison, rabbit, duck, or fish work well.

Feed only the novel protein for 8-12 weeks while keeping a detailed symptom journal. Document itching levels, ear health, stool quality, energy levels, and anything else you notice. If symptoms improve significantly during the elimination phase, you can try reintroducing other ingredients one at a time to pinpoint triggers.

One important note: many Shih Tzus who react to chicken in commercial kibble do fine with raw chicken. The processing that commercial foods undergo alters protein structure, potentially making it more allergenic. Raw chicken in its natural form may not trigger the same reactions. Before assuming your Shih Tzu has a chicken allergy, try raw chicken from a single source.

Daily Portion Guidelines

Shih Tzus typically need 2-2.5% of body weight in raw food daily for weight maintenance. Less active senior dogs might do well on 1.5-2%, while younger, more active Shih Tzus might need closer to 3%.

Here's a reference for typical adult Shih Tzu portions:

  • 9 lb Shih Tzu: 2.9-3.6 oz daily
  • 12 lb Shih Tzu: 3.8-4.8 oz daily
  • 16 lb Shih Tzu: 5.1-6.4 oz daily

These amounts assume a typical adult with moderate activity. Adjust based on your individual dog's body condition. You should be able to feel (but not see) ribs. A visible waist when viewed from above indicates healthy weight. If your Shih Tzu is gaining weight, reduce portions slightly. If ribs are becoming prominent, increase portions.

Shih Tzu puppies need more food relative to their size—typically 5-8% of body weight daily, split across three to four meals. Growing puppies have enormous energy needs for development. Never severely restrict food for puppies.

The breed's tendency toward obesity makes monitoring weight crucial. Weigh your Shih Tzu monthly and adjust portions accordingly. Those few extra pounds put stress on their flat faces and can worsen breathing problems.

Best Protein Sources

Shih Tzus generally do well on poultry-based diets, but variety matters. Rotate between different proteins to ensure comprehensive nutrition and prevent the development of new sensitivities.

Chicken is widely available, affordable, and usually palatable. Dark meat provides more fat for energy and coat health. Chicken necks, wings, and thighs all work for Shih Tzus who can handle whole bones.

Turkey offers similar nutrition to chicken with different amino acid profiles. Ground turkey works well for Shih Tzus who prefer softer textures.

Lamb is often recommended for dogs with suspected food sensitivities. It's less commonly used in commercial foods, making it a good novel protein option. Lamb is slightly fattier than chicken, which many Shih Tzus find appealing.

Duck is another novel protein that works for sensitive dogs. It's readily available and provides good nutrition. Duck feet are excellent for dental health and provide glucosamine for joints.

Rabbit is one of the most hypoallergenic proteins available. It's lean and provides a nice change from poultry and red meat. Rabbit can be harder to find and more expensive, but some owners keep it as a rotation protein.

Fish provides omega-3 fatty acids that benefit skin and coat. Sardines, mackerel, and salmon offer different nutrient profiles. Feed fish 2-3 times per week as part of rotation.

Transitioning Your Shih Tzu

Shih Tzus typically accept raw food more readily than some breeds. Their food motivation and somewhat indiscriminate appetites work in your favor during transitions.

Start by offering small amounts of raw as high-value treats. Most Shih Tzus dive in immediately—if anything, you may need to slow them down to prevent gulping. After a week of treat introduction, start replacing part of one meal with raw.

The transition typically takes 2-3 weeks. Watch stool quality—if it becomes too loose, reduce organ meat or increase bone content slightly. Chalky, hard stools indicate too much bone. Adjust ratios until stools are firm but not hard, and easy to pick up.

Temperature affects acceptance. Room temperature food is more aromatic than cold food straight from the fridge. Warm food slightly if your Shih Tzu seems reluctant—this is especially helpful during initial transitions.

Some Shih Tzus are enthusiastic gulpers. If your dog tends to swallow food without chewing, consider feeding pre-cut pieces rather than whole chunks. This reduces choking risk and helps with digestion. Bone content is still important, so include ground bone or soft meaty bones if whole bones aren't appropriate.

Special Concerns for the Breed

Ear infections are common in Shih Tzus due to their floppy ears and tendency toward allergies. Raw feeding can help by reducing allergic triggers, but ear cleaning remains important. Check and clean ears weekly.

Eye issues require attention with this breed. Their prominent eyes are prone to irritation and injury. Food quality doesn't directly affect eye health, but adequate vitamin A supports eye function. Raw liver provides vitamin A in safe amounts.

Respiratory concerns mean you should avoid feeding large meals that might contribute to bloat risk. Split food into two meals rather than one large serving. Don't exercise your Shih Tzu vigorously immediately before or after eating.

Dental disease is common in the breed. The small mouth and crowded teeth promote plaque buildup. Raw meaty bones provide natural teeth cleaning. Chicken necks, wings, and feet work well for most Shih Tzus. If your dog has dental work or missing teeth, ground bone still provides some benefit.

Frequently Asked Questions

What foods should Shih Tzus avoid on a raw diet?

Avoid onions, garlic, grapes, raisins, chocolate, xylitol, and avocado—these are toxic to dogs. Cooked bones of any kind are dangerous and must never be fed. High-mercury fish should be limited. Pork should be fed cautiously due to parasite risks. Salt and seasonings should be avoided. The core raw diet of meat, bone, and organs naturally avoids these problems.

How does raw feeding help Shih Tzu skin problems?

Commercial foods contain allergens and inflammatory ingredients that trigger skin reactions in sensitive Shih Tzus. Raw feeding eliminates these triggers and provides omega-3s that reduce inflammation. The bioavailable nutrients in raw food support skin barrier function. Many owners report significant improvement in itching, redness, and coat quality within 4-8 weeks of switching.

Can Shih Tzus have food allergies like humans?

Yes, dogs can develop food allergies and sensitivities, and Shih Tzus are prone to them. True food allergies involve immune system reactions to specific proteins. The most common culprits are chicken, beef, dairy, wheat, soy, and corn—all common in commercial foods. Environmental allergies also affect Shih Tzus but are unrelated to diet.

How much should I feed my Shih Tzu puppy?

Shih Tzu puppies need 5-8% of body weight daily, divided across three to four meals. A 4-pound Shih Tzu puppy needs about 3.2-5.1 ounces of food per day. Puppies burn enormous energy growing, and adequate nutrition is essential for proper development. Use our portion calculator for specific recommendations based on your puppy's current weight.

Are Shih Tzus prone to obesity?

Yes, Shih Tzus gain weight easily, especially as they age and become less active. Extra weight puts strain on their respiratory systems and joints. Raw feeding helps because the high protein content maintains lean muscle while the absence of carbs reduces fat storage. Portion control and regular weight monitoring are essential for keeping Shih Tzus at healthy weights.

What supplements does my Shih Tzu need on raw food?

Most Shih Tzus don't need supplements if eating a balanced raw diet. For skin and coat issues, fish oil or whole fish boosts omega-3s. For joint health, green-lipped mussel powder helps. Probiotics support digestion during transitions. Discuss supplements with your vet if your Shih Tzu has specific health concerns.

How do I safely introduce bones to my Shih Tzu?

Start with appropriately sized bones—chicken necks or wings work well for most Shih Tzus. Supervise closely, especially initially. Some Shih Tzus gulp without chewing; if yours does, consider ground bone instead of whole bones. Never feed weight-bearing bones from large animals, as these are too dense and can damage teeth. Remove bones if your dog seems to be struggling or trying to swallow large pieces.
