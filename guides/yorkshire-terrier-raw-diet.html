Yorkies are among the most beloved toy breeds, prized for their gorgeous silky coats and spirited personalities. Those flowing locks and feisty attitudes require proper nutrition to maintain—and raw feeding delivers in ways commercial foods simply can't match.

Yorkie Nutritional Needs

Adult Yorkshire Terriers typically weigh between 4 and 7 pounds, with show dogs often at the smaller end of that range. Despite their tiny size, Yorkies are terriers at heart—alert, curious, and surprisingly athletic. They need quality nutrition to fuel their big personalities.

The breed originated in 19th-century Scotland and Yorkshire, where they worked as ratters in textile mills and coal mines. Despite their glamorous show-dog appearance today, Yorkies retain the hardy constitution of their working ancestors. They tend to be robust and healthy when properly cared for, with lifespans often reaching 15 years or more.

Yorkies have fast metabolisms, much like other toy breeds. They burn through calories quickly and need regular meals to maintain stable blood sugar. Their small stomachs mean each meal is necessarily small, making nutrient density critical.

The most striking feature of many Yorkies is their coat—long, silky, and prone to tangling without proper care. Coat quality depends heavily on diet. Protein provides the building blocks for strong hair strands. Fats contribute shine and manageability. Vitamins and minerals support follicle health and pigment production. A raw diet provides these nutrients in their most bioavailable forms.

Managing Hypoglycemia Risk

Like other toy breeds, Yorkies face real risk of hypoglycemia—dangerously low blood sugar that can cause weakness, disorientation, tremors, and seizures. Understanding and preventing this condition is essential for every Yorkie owner.

Yorkie puppies are especially vulnerable. A puppy's liver is still developing and can't store glycogen efficiently. Stress from new environments, missing a meal, or even excitement can trigger hypoglycemic episodes. Never skip meals for a Yorkie puppy, and keep a glucose source readily available.

The transition to raw feeding actually helps stabilize blood sugar compared to high-carbohydrate kibble. Protein and fat digest more slowly than starches, providing steady energy rather than glucose spikes. However, you still need to feed frequently enough to prevent blood sugar crashes.

Adult Yorkies typically do well with two meals per day, but three smaller meals is even better for maintaining stable energy. Space meals no more than 8-10 hours apart during waking hours. Keep honey or glucose gel in your pocket for emergencies—if you notice wobbliness, confusion, or weakness, rub a small amount on your Yorkie's gums immediately.

Signs of hypoglycemia include lethargy, confusion, wobbly gait, tremors, and in severe cases, collapse or seizures. If your Yorkie shows these symptoms, act fast. Give sugar, then food, then contact your vet if episodes are severe or recurrent.

Dental Health for Yorkies

Dental disease is epidemic in small breed dogs, and Yorkies are particularly susceptible. Their small mouths pack 42 teeth (the same number as large breeds) into a tiny space, leading to crowding, misalignment, and accelerated plaque buildup. By age three, most Yorkies show signs of periodontal disease.

Raw feeding provides significant dental benefits. Chewing raw meaty bones scrapes away plaque mechanically in a way that brushing alone can't match. The act of tearing and crunching stimulates gums and keeps oral tissues healthy. Many Yorkie owners notice dramatically improved dental health after just months of raw feeding.

Not all bones are appropriate for Yorkies, though. Their tiny mouths and relatively small teeth need appropriately sized options. Chicken necks from small birds work well. Quail bones are even more manageable. Turkey necks are larger and may be too challenging for some Yorkies.

For Yorkies who struggle with whole bones due to missing teeth or dental work, ground bone added to meals still provides some dental benefit from the mechanical action of eating. It doesn't replace whole-bone chewing, but it's better than nothing.

Start bone chewing gradually. Some Yorkies dive right in; others need encouragement. Offer bones at the same time each day to build a habit. Supervise closely, especially initially, to ensure your Yorkie doesn't try to swallow large pieces whole.

Coat Health Through Nutrition

The Yorkie's signature coat is actually hair, not fur. This means it's continuously growing like human hair, requiring constant nutrition to maintain quality. Many show Yorkies are fed raw diets specifically to achieve the coat condition that judges look for.

Protein is the foundation of coat health. Hair is made of keratin, an animal protein. Without adequate dietary protein, the coat becomes brittle, thin, and loses its shine. Raw meat provides complete amino acid profiles that support hair growth and strength.

Omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids are essential for coat quality. Omega-3s (EPA and DHA) reduce inflammation and support follicle health. Omega-6s (found in animal fats) contribute to coat softness and sheen. Raw meat and fish provide these fats in their natural, bioavailable forms.

Zinc plays a role in coat pigmentation and skin health. Organ meats, particularly liver, are excellent zinc sources. Some Yorkies with chronic skin issues benefit from additional zinc supplementation.

Vitamin A supports skin cell turnover. While vitamin A toxicity is possible from excessive liver consumption, moderate amounts (about 5% of the total diet as liver) provide beneficial amounts without risk.

Biotin and B vitamins support overall coat condition. Egg yolks are excellent sources of biotin and provide healthy fats. Adding a raw egg yolk to meals a few times per week boosts coat nutrition significantly.

Daily Portion Guidelines

Yorkies need 2-3% of their body weight in raw food daily, depending on activity level, age, and metabolism. A typical adult Yorkie might weigh 5 pounds, requiring about 1.6 to 2.4 ounces of raw food per day.

These tiny portions mean precision matters enormously. A kitchen scale that measures in ounces or grams is essential equipment for any Yorkie owner feeding raw. Eyeballing portions with this breed invites weight management problems.

Monitor body condition weekly. You should be able to feel (but not see) your Yorkie's ribs. They should have a visible waist when viewed from above. If ribs are prominent, add more food. If the waist is disappearing, reduce portions.

Yorkie puppies need significantly more—typically 5-8% of body weight daily, split across three to four meals. Growing puppies need fuel for development, and their small stomachs can't hold enough food for adequate growth if meals are too spaced out.

Senior Yorkies may need portion adjustments. Less active older dogs often need slightly reduced portions to prevent weight gain. Some seniors with health issues need increased protein to prevent muscle loss. Adjust based on your individual dog's condition and consult your vet if you're unsure.

Best Foods for Yorkies

Yorkies often thrive on chicken-based diets, which many find highly palatable. Chicken thighs provide good fat content along with protein. Chicken neck or wing provides bone for dental health and calcium.

Turkey is another excellent option, similar in composition to chicken but offering variety. Many owners rotate between chicken and turkey to provide diverse nutrient profiles.

Beef provides iron and zinc in highly available forms. Ground beef with visible fat works well for Yorkies who struggle with chunkier cuts. Look for 80/20 or 85/15 ground beef.

Fish like sardines (raw, whole) provides omega-3 fatty acids for coat health. Feed 1-2 times per week. Salmon is another good option, though mercury concerns mean it should be fed less frequently than smaller fish.

Organ meats should comprise about 10% of the total diet, with half of that being liver. Chicken liver, beef liver, and lamb liver all work. Kidney, spleen, and heart provide variety in secreting organs.

Eggs (whole, raw) provide protein, biotin, and healthy fats. Egg yolks are especially beneficial for coat health. Most Yorkies can handle a whole egg several times per week.

Transitioning Tips

Yorkies can be selective eaters, so a gradual transition often works better than an abrupt switch. Start by offering small amounts of raw as treats. Most Yorkies can't resist meat, so this initial step usually goes smoothly.

After a week of treat introduction, start replacing one meal partially with raw. Watch for any digestive upset over the next few days. Loose stools may indicate too much organ meat or too little bone. Adjust ratios accordingly.

Temperature affects acceptance. Yorkies often prefer food at room temperature rather than straight from the refrigerator. Cold food has less aroma, and these discerning eaters notice.

If your Yorkie seems reluctant, try different protein sources. Some Yorkies strongly prefer chicken; others love beef. You might need to experiment to find your dog's favorites.

Hand feeding helps build positive associations and lets you monitor exactly how much your Yorkie eats. It also provides bonding time that Yorkies often crave.

Once your Yorkie is accepting raw readily, you can move to full raw meals. Many Yorkies do best with three smaller meals rather than two larger ones. Check out our beginner's guide for more transition details.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Yorkies handle raw bones without choking?

Most adult Yorkies can handle appropriately sized raw bones. Chicken necks from small birds, quail bones, and chicken wings work well. Always supervise initial bone chewing. If your Yorkie tends to gulp food without much chewing, start with ground bone instead of whole bones until you can assess their chewing habits.

How does raw feeding improve Yorkie coat quality?

Raw diets provide bioavailable nutrients that support coat growth. Protein provides building blocks for keratin. Omega-3s reduce inflammation in skin and follicles. Zinc and vitamin A support pigment and cell turnover. Many Yorkie owners report coat improvements within 2-3 months of switching to raw. The coat becomes shinier, stronger, and less prone to breakage.

What if my Yorkie won't eat raw food?

Try different proteins—some Yorkies have strong preferences. Warm the food slightly to enhance aroma. Add a splash of bone broth (no onion or garlic). Try hand feeding or offering food in a puzzle toy. You can also try mixing a small amount of raw with their current food and gradually increasing the ratio over 2-3 weeks.

How do I prevent hypoglycemia in my Yorkie?

Feed regular meals—never skip feedings. Keep to a consistent schedule. Have emergency glucose available (honey, glucose gel). Feed adequate fat for sustained energy. Monitor for early warning signs (lethargy, wobbling). Puppies especially need frequent meals. Discuss with your vet if your Yorkie has recurrent hypoglycemic episodes.

How much should I feed my Yorkie puppy?

Yorkie puppies need 5-8% of body weight daily, split across three to four meals. A 2-pound Yorkie puppy needs about 1.6-2.6 ounces of food per day total. Growing puppies burn enormous energy and need adequate fuel for development. Never restrict puppy food severely. Use our portion calculator for specific recommendations based on your puppy's weight and age.

Can Yorkies eat raw eggs for biotin?

Yes, raw eggs are safe and beneficial. The biotin in egg yolks supports coat health. The avidin in egg whites binds biotin, but not enough in typical feeding amounts to cause deficiency. Some owners feed only yolks if concerned, though whole eggs are generally fine. Yorkies typically enjoy eggs mixed into their regular meals.

What supplements does my Yorkie need on a raw diet?

Most Yorkies don't need supplements if eating a properly balanced raw diet. For coat health, adding raw egg yolks a few times per week provides biotin. For skin issues, fish oil (small amount) or whole fish like sardines boosts omega-3s. Probiotics can help during transition. Discuss specific supplements with your vet if your Yorkie has particular health concerns.
