BARF Diet for Small Breeds: Complete Implementation Guide

The BARF diet—Biologically Appropriate Raw Food, or sometimes Bones and Raw Food—adds plant matter to the raw meat foundation. For small breed owners, BARF offers a way to incorporate vegetables for fiber, antioxidants, and variety while maintaining the benefits of fresh, uncooked nutrition. This guide shows you exactly how to implement BARF for your small dog.

What Is the BARF Diet?

BARF was popularized by Dr. Ian Billinghurst, an Australian veterinarian who observed that dogs thrive on diets closer to what their wild ancestors ate. The key insight is that wolves and wild dogs don't just eat muscle meat—they consume the entire herbivore prey, including stomach contents (partially digested plants), organs, blood, and bone.

The BARF model aims to replicate this complete prey approach while making it practical for modern home feeders. In addition to meat, bone, and organs, BARF includes vegetables that simulate the plant material wild dogs would consume via prey stomachs.

The original BARF ratios include 10-20% vegetables, with the remaining 80-90% being animal-based. Some BARF feeders include small amounts of fruit, seeds, or nuts, though these additions are optional and should be minimal.

For small breeds specifically, BARF can provide benefits like improved stool consistency (fiber helps), more diverse gut bacteria, and additional antioxidants from vegetable sources. However, the animal-based portion remains the foundation—vegetables supplement rather than replace meat.

BARF vs. Prey Model

Two main approaches dominate raw feeding: BARF and Prey Model. Understanding the differences helps you decide which approach suits your small dog.

Prey Model aims to replicate an entire animal with ratios of approximately 80% muscle meat, 10% bone, 5% liver, and 5% other secreting organs. No plant matter is included. Followers argue that dogs have short digestive tracts designed for meat, not plants, and that vegetables provide minimal benefit.

BARF includes plant matter—typically 10-20% vegetables—along with the animal-based components. Proponents argue that wild dogs consume plants indirectly through prey stomachs and directly when hungry or deficient. BARF also tends to include seeds, nuts, or eggshells for additional nutrients.

For small breeds, both approaches can work well. Some small dogs with sensitive digestion do better on Prey Model, as they can't process much plant matter efficiently. Others benefit from the fiber and antioxidants in vegetables. Many experienced feeders use elements of both, adjusting based on how their individual dog thrives.

The beginner's guide covers both models in more detail if you want to explore further.

Understanding BARF Ratios

The standard BARF ratios for adult dogs are approximately:

  • 70-80% muscle meat (including heart, which is muscular meat)
  • 10% bone
  • 5-10% liver
  • 5% other secreting organs (kidney, spleen, brain, etc.)
  • 10-20% vegetables

These percentages are guidelines, not strict rules. Your dog doesn't need a calculator at every meal—as long as ratios average out over a week or two, you're doing well. Variety is more important than precision.

Here's how the components break down:

Muscle meat is the foundation. Include a variety: chicken, turkey, beef, lamb, pork, venison, rabbit, duck. Heart counts as muscle meat and provides taurine, an essential amino acid for heart health. Include 5-10% heart in your overall ratio.

Bone provides calcium and phosphorus. Without adequate bone, you risk calcium deficiency. Meat alone is too high in phosphorus relative to calcium. Raw meaty bones provide bone in the correct ratios. If your small dog can't handle whole bones, ground bone works too.

Liver is essential and should never be skipped. It provides vitamin A, copper, iron, and many other nutrients. Feed about 5% liver maximum—more causes vitamin A toxicity. Some dogs love liver; others turn up their noses. If your dog refuses liver, consider adding liver powder as a supplement.

Other secreting organs include kidney, spleen, brain, pancreas, testicles, thymus, and others. These provide different nutrient profiles than liver. Variety in secreting organs ensures comprehensive nutrition. Feed about 5% total of non-liver secreting organs.

Vegetables provide fiber, antioxidants, and some phytonutrients. Leafy greens like kale, spinach, and romaine are excellent. Carrots, zucchini, broccoli, and cucumber add variety. Keep vegetables to 10-20% of total intake—more than this and your small dog may struggle to digest plant matter efficiently.

Small Breed Adjustments

Small breeds have some specific considerations when implementing BARF.

Bone sizing. This is critical for small dogs. A Great Dane can handle a chicken back; a Chihuahua cannot. Use smaller bones like chicken necks from small birds, quail, or finely ground bone. Turkey necks are usually too large for toy breeds. Chicken wings work for many small breeds, but supervise closely.

Vegetable tolerance. Small dogs have faster digestive transit times and sometimes can't process much vegetable matter. Some toy breeds do better with vegetables at the lower end of the range (10%) rather than higher amounts. Watch stool consistency—if vegetables seem to cause gas or loose stools, reduce the amount.

Organ meat proportioning. With tiny portions, it's easy to overfeed liver by accident. A few extra grams of liver represent a much larger percentage of the diet in a 5-pound dog than in a 50-pound dog. Use a kitchen scale and track weekly intake to ensure proper ratios.

Dental considerations. Small breeds are prone to dental disease, and raw bones provide excellent dental cleaning. However, some small dogs have dental work or missing teeth that make bone chewing difficult. For these dogs, finely ground bone or bone broth provides some calcium without the chewing challenge.

Energy density. Small dogs need calorie-dense food because their stomachs are tiny. Make sure your BARF meals are protein and fat-rich rather than bulked up with vegetables. Vegetables add fiber but few calories—a too-vegetable-heavy diet leaves small dogs hungry despite adequate volume.

Weekly Meal Plan Example

Here's an example weekly plan for a small dog eating 3 ounces daily (adjust based on your dog's actual needs):

Day 1:

  • 1.5 oz chicken thigh with bone
  • 0.3 oz chicken liver
  • 0.15 oz kidney
  • 0.3 oz pureed kale
  • 0.15 oz ground chicken heart

Day 2:

  • 1.2 oz ground turkey
  • 0.3 oz turkey heart
  • 0.2 oz chicken liver
  • 0.2 oz grated carrots
  • 0.3 oz ground bone

Day 3:

  • 1.5 oz beef (ground, fatty)
  • 0.3 oz beef liver
  • 0.2 oz pureed spinach
  • 0.2 oz beef kidney

Day 4:

  • 1.2 oz lamb (ground)
  • 0.3 oz chicken neck (with meat)
  • 0.25 oz chicken liver
  • 0.3 oz zucchini
  • 0.15 oz heart

Day 5:

  • 1.4 oz duck (ground)
  • 0.3 oz duck liver
  • 0.2 oz kidney
  • 0.3 oz broccoli (tiny amount, steamed briefly then cooled)

Day 6:

  • 1.5 oz rabbit (ground)
  • 0.3 oz chicken liver
  • 0.3 oz cucumber
  • 0.15 oz heart
  • 0.25 oz ground bone

Day 7:

  • 1.3 oz sardines (whole, raw)
  • 1.0 oz chicken thigh
  • 0.3 oz chicken liver
  • 0.4 oz kale

This rotation provides variety in proteins, ensures adequate organ meats, and includes vegetables most days. Adjust portion sizes to match your dog's actual daily needs.

Vegetable Guide

Not all vegetables are created equal for dogs. Here's what works well in BARF diets:

Excellent choices (feed freely):

  • Kale (nutrient-dense, high in calcium)
  • Spinach (in moderation—contains oxalates)
  • Romaine lettuce
  • Carrots (grated or pureed for digestibility)
  • Zucchini
  • Cucumber
  • Pumpkin (plain, not spiced)

Acceptable in small amounts:

  • Broccoli (can cause gas in some dogs)
  • Cauliflower
  • Celery
  • Green beans
  • Bell peppers

Avoid:

  • Onions, garlic, leeks, chives (toxic)
  • Corn (difficult to digest whole)
  • Mushrooms (some species toxic)
  • Avocado (contains persin)

Always puree, grate, or blend vegetables for small dogs. Their digestive systems can't break down whole plant cells effectively. Processing vegetables makes the nutrients accessible.

Supplement Checklist

A properly formulated BARF diet should meet most nutritional needs. However, some supplements provide insurance:

Fish oil (if not feeding fish 2-3x weekly): Provides EPA and DHA omega-3s. Start with small amounts—too much causes loose stools. One-quarter teaspoon for small dogs is usually sufficient.

Vitamin E: Works synergistically with omega-3s. One small capsule per week provides antioxidant support.

Probiotics: Support digestive health and diverse gut bacteria. Particularly helpful during transitions.

Kelp powder: Provides iodine and trace minerals. Use sparingly—too much iodine can cause thyroid issues.

Green-lipped mussel: Provides glucosamine and chondroitin for joint health. Particularly valuable for breeds prone to joint problems.

Most small dogs don't need additional calcium if eating adequate bone content. If you're concerned about calcium, discuss supplementation with your vet.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can small dogs digest vegetables in the BARF diet?

Yes, but they process them less efficiently than meat. Small dogs have faster digestive transit times and shorter intestines. Pureeing, grating, or blending vegetables makes nutrients more accessible. Keep vegetables to 10-20% of total intake—more than this and small dogs may struggle. Watch for gas or loose stools as signs vegetables aren't being digested well.

What's the difference between BARF and Prey Model?

BARF includes 10-20% vegetables, simulating plant material wild dogs consume via prey stomachs. Prey Model is strictly animal-based with no vegetables. BARF proponents value vegetables for fiber and antioxidants; Prey Model proponents argue dogs are carnivores that don't need plants. Either approach works—many feeders use elements of both. See our beginner's guide for more details.

How do I know if my BARF ratios are correct?

Track intake over a week or two. As long as ratios average out roughly (70-80% muscle meat, 10% bone, 5% liver, 5% other organs, 10-20% vegetables), you're doing well. You don't need precision at every meal. Stool quality is a good indicator: firm, easy-to-pick-up stools suggest good balance. Loose stools may indicate too much organ meat or vegetables. Chalky stools indicate too much bone.

Can I feed BARF to puppies?

Yes, puppies can eat BARF, but they need more total food (5-8% of body weight daily) and may need vegetables at the lower end of the range (10%) or omitted entirely. Growing puppies have high protein and calcium needs that should come primarily from meat and bone. Discuss puppy BARF with your vet or breeder for specific guidance on portion sizes and timing.

What if my small dog won't eat vegetables?

Many small dogs aren't enthusiastic about vegetables. Try pureeing them and mixing into meat. You can also try different vegetables—some dogs prefer sweeter options like carrots. If your dog absolutely refuses vegetables, you can omit them entirely and follow more of a Prey Model approach. Dogs don't require vegetables as long as they're getting balanced nutrition from animal sources.

How do I handle vegetables safely for raw feeding?

Wash vegetables thoroughly. Organic is ideal but not required. Puree or blend vegetables for small dogs—whole or large pieces are difficult to digest. Store prepared vegetables separately from raw meat to prevent cross-contamination. Use vegetables within 3-5 days when fresh, or freeze portions for longer storage.

Can I include fruit in BARF?

Fruit is optional in BARF. Some feeders include small amounts of berries, melon, or apple (no seeds). Fruit contains sugars and should be limited, especially for small dogs prone to weight gain. Many BARF feeders omit fruit entirely and focus on vegetables. If you include fruit, keep it to less than 5% of total intake.
