How to Transition Your Small Dog to Raw Food: Step-by-Step Guide

Switching your small dog from kibble or canned food to raw is one of the most rewarding things you can do for their health. But the transition requires patience and understanding. Your dog's digestive system needs time to adapt, and every dog responds differently. This guide walks you through the process step by step, so you know exactly what to expect and how to handle any hiccups along the way.

Choosing Your Transition Method

Two main approaches exist for transitioning to raw: the quick switch and the gradual method. Both can work, but one may suit your dog better than the other.

The quick switch involves stopping kibble entirely and switching to raw food all at once. Some dogs handle this remarkably well—within 24 hours, their digestion adjusts and they thrive on raw food. This approach works best for dogs with robust digestive systems, no history of food sensitivities, and no current digestive issues.

The gradual method involves replacing kibble with raw food over 1-3 weeks. This approach suits sensitive dogs, those with digestive histories, and small breeds prone to stomach upset. It also lets you identify which ingredients cause any adverse reactions.

Small dogs often benefit from the gradual approach. Their digestive systems can be more sensitive, and their small size means there's less margin for error if digestive upset occurs. The gradual method also helps you monitor stool quality as you introduce different components—muscle meat, organs, and bone.

Consider your dog's current health when choosing a method. Dogs with chronic digestive issues, food allergies, or recent illness should use the gradual method. Healthy dogs with no sensitivity history might thrive with the quick switch. If you're unsure, the gradual method is always safer. For breed-specific transition tips, see our guides on Chihuahuas, French Bulldogs, and other small breed feeding basics.

Week One: Getting Started

The first week sets the foundation for your transition. Go slowly and observe carefully—this week teaches you what your dog can handle.

Days 1-3: Introduction

Offer a small amount of raw food as a treat or side dish alongside your dog's normal meals. Choose a protein source distinctly different from their current food. If they've been eating chicken-based kibble, try beef or rabbit. The goal is to make raw food seem exciting, not threatening.

Feed 5-10 small pieces of raw meat (about the size of your thumbnail) during this period. Observe for any adverse reactions over the next 24 hours. Most dogs show no reaction at all. Some might seem more interested in their regular food initially—that's normal.

Days 4-7: First Meal Replacement

If days 1-3 went smoothly, start replacing one meal with raw. Use a simple meal: muscle meat with about 5% organ content (save the bone content for later). Feed about one-quarter of what you'd normally give for that meal.

Expect some changes in stool quality. Loose stools or increased stool volume in the first few days of raw introduction is normal. The digestive system is adapting to new bacteria and different nutrient profiles. If stools become watery or contain blood, pause the transition and consult your vet.

Keep your dog well-hydrated during this period. Raw food contains more moisture than kibble, but some dogs drink less when eating raw. Monitor water intake and encourage drinking if necessary.

Week Two: Building Confidence

If week one went smoothly, week two is about expanding your dog's raw diet repertoire and adjusting portions.

Days 8-10: Expanding to Half Meals

Now replace half of one meal with raw food. Increase the raw portion to about half of what that meal would normally be. Continue watching stool quality closely. Some dogs do perfectly at this stage; others might experience temporary loose stools.

Begin introducing bone content gradually. Add 5% ground bone or finely cut bone-in pieces to the muscle meat. Bone helps firm up stools, so it's often the missing ingredient when stools remain loose. Adjust bone content based on what you observe.

Days 11-14: Near-Full Replacement

Replace three-quarters of that one meal with raw food. Continue the same protein source, or begin rotating if digestion has been stable. This rotation provides variety in nutrients and prevents sensitivity development.

Consider adding variety in organ content as well. If you've been feeding liver, you can now add other secreting organs: kidney, spleen, or brain. These provide different nutrient profiles that complement muscle meat.

By the end of week two, your dog should be eating approximately one full raw meal daily (if transitioning to two meals daily on raw) or having one raw meal that replaces one of their regular meals entirely. The other meal(s) should still be their regular food.

Week Three: Going Full Raw

Week three is where the transition completes for most dogs. By the end of this week, your small dog should be fully on raw food.

Days 15-18: Full Single Meal Replacement

Replace one entire meal with raw food. Feed a complete meal with appropriate bone and organ content. For most small dogs, a complete meal consists of approximately 80% muscle meat, 10% bone, 5% liver, and 5% other secreting organs.

Monitor energy levels, coat quality, and overall demeanor. Many owners notice improvements within the first few weeks: shinier coats, better breath, more regular stools, and increased energy. These positive changes reinforce that the transition is working.

Days 19-21: Complete Transition

Replace the second meal with raw food. Now your dog is fully transitioned. Fine-tune portion sizes based on your dog's appetite and body condition.

During this final week, establish your protein rotation schedule. Most dogs thrive on 3-5 different protein sources rotated over weeks or months. Some owners prefer daily rotation; others rotate every few days or weekly. Find what works for your dog and your schedule.

The complete transition takes 3-4 weeks for most dogs. Some sensitive dogs need 4-6 weeks. That's perfectly fine—there's no rush. The goal is a healthy, happy dog on a sustainable raw diet, not speed.

Understanding Stool Changes

Your dog's stool will change significantly during and after the transition. Understanding these changes helps you interpret what's normal versus what needs attention.

Normal Transitions:

During the first days of raw introduction, stools might become slightly softer or more frequent. This is normal as the gut adapts to new bacteria and different nutrients. The odor often decreases significantly—many owners notice much less smell from raw-fed dog stools compared to kibble stools.

Raw-fed stools are typically smaller than kibble stools because dogs absorb more nutrition from raw food. Less waste means less stool. This is a good sign—it means your dog is using the food efficiently.

Stool color changes are normal. Raw-fed stools are often darker (due to higher meat content) and may have different textures based on bone content. Very pale, chalky stools suggest too much bone. Very loose stools suggest too little bone or too much organ meat.

Signs of Problems:

Watery diarrhea that persists more than 48 hours needs attention. Temporary diarrhea might occur during transitions, but prolonged diarrhea risks dehydration, especially in small dogs. Blood in stool (not just a small amount after bone introduction) requires veterinary consultation.

Greasy, mucous-covered stools sometimes indicate pancreas issues or food sensitivities. If you see these consistently, consult your vet. Undigested food in stools (beyond whole vegetables that might pass through) suggests portions are too large or the dog is eating too fast.

If stools remain loose after three weeks on full raw, consider adjusting bone content upward. Add 5% more ground bone and monitor for changes. Too little bone is a more common cause of loose stools than too much.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Every dog responds differently to the transition. Here are solutions to common problems you might encounter.

Refusing to Eat Raw Food

If your dog turns up their nose at raw, try warming it slightly (not hot, just above refrigerator temperature). Add a splash of bone broth (no onion or garlic) for flavor. Offer different proteins—some dogs simply prefer certain meats. Hand-feeding creates positive associations. Hunger usually wins within 24-48 hours, but never starve a toy breed into eating.

Excessive Hunger on Raw

Some dogs seem perpetually hungry on raw food. This might mean portions are too small, or it might reflect better digestion (raw food digests faster than kibble, so dogs might ask for food sooner). Increase portions slightly if stools are firm and ribs aren't showing. If portions are adequate, accept that some dogs are simply enthusiastic eaters.

Not Enough Time to Chew

Some dogs gulp food without chewing. This can cause digestive upset and increases choking risk. Try feeding in a muffin tin or on a plate rather than in a bowl to slow eating. Larger pieces require more chewing. Some owners use puzzle feeders to extend mealtime.

Transition Setbacks

If your dog gets sick, experiences stress, or has digestive upset during the transition, pause and let them recover. Resume at a lower raw portion once they're better. Setbacks happen—there's no penalty for taking extra time. For more detailed troubleshooting, see our food safety guide.

When to Consult Your Vet

Most transitions proceed smoothly without veterinary involvement. But certain situations warrant professional guidance.

Consult your vet if your dog has pre-existing health conditions, especially digestive disorders, pancreatitis, or kidney disease. These conditions require careful diet management that your vet can help coordinate with raw feeding.

Seek veterinary care for diarrhea lasting more than 48 hours, especially in small dogs who risk dehydration quickly. Vomiting that persists, lethargy, refusal to eat for more than 24 hours (in adult dogs), or blood in stool all warrant veterinary attention.

Puppies transitioning to raw should be monitored closely, as they risk hypoglycemia and have different nutritional needs than adults. Consult our puppy feeding guide for specific guidance on young dogs.

If your vet seems opposed to raw feeding, seek a second opinion from a vet experienced with raw diets. Not all vets receive nutrition education, and many oppose raw feeding based on theoretical risks rather than clinical experience. Look for integrative or functional medicine veterinarians who take a more holistic view of nutrition.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I do a quick switch or gradual transition for my small dog?

For healthy small dogs with no digestive sensitivity history, the quick switch often works fine. For toy breeds, small dogs with sensitive stomachs, those with food allergies, or dogs switching from grain-free or prescription diets, the gradual method is safer. When in doubt, go gradual.

My dog refuses to eat raw food. What should I do?

Try warming the food slightly, adding bone broth for flavor, or offering a different protein. Hand-feed to create positive associations. Be patient—most dogs accept raw within 24-72 hours of hunger. Never starve a small dog into eating; hypoglycemia is dangerous in toy and small breeds.

How long does the transition take?

Most dogs fully transition in 2-3 weeks. Sensitive dogs may need 4-6 weeks. There's no deadline—take the time your individual dog needs. Rushing leads to digestive upset that extends the transition.

What stool changes are normal during the transition?

Stools might become slightly softer initially, then firm up as bone content increases. Raw-fed stools are typically smaller, darker, and less odorous than kibble stools. Very loose stools suggest too little bone or too much organ meat. Chalky white stools indicate too much bone. Check our portion guide for ratio adjustments.

Can I mix raw food with kibble during the transition?

Technically yes, but it's not ideal. Dogs produce different digestive enzymes for raw versus processed food, and mixing can cause incomplete digestion of both. Some owners do successfully mix during gradual transitions, but complete raw feeding generally produces better results once fully transitioned.

What if my dog gets diarrhea during the transition?

Some loose stools are normal in the first few days. If diarrhea persists more than 48 hours, pause the transition and return to normal food until digestion stabilizes. Then resume at a lower raw portion. If diarrhea contains blood or is severe, consult your vet immediately.

Should I give probiotics during the transition?

Probiotics can support digestion during transitions, especially for sensitive dogs. Small amounts of plain kefir or fermented vegetables provide natural probiotics. Commercial probiotic supplements designed for dogs are also options. They're not required but can smooth the transition.

My small dog seems hungry all the time on raw food. Is this normal?

Raw food digests faster than kibble, so some dogs ask to eat more frequently. Make sure portions are adequate—you should feel (not see) ribs but not be able to press hard to find them. If portions seem correct, your dog may simply be an enthusiastic eater. Some owners feed three smaller meals to help small dogs feel satisfied.
