Maltese Raw Diet: Feeding Guide for Sensitive Tummies

Maltese dogs are the aristocrats of the toy breed world—elegant, white-furred companions who have graced the laps of nobility for millennia. That stunning pure white coat and those big dark eyes come with some significant care requirements. Many Maltese owners struggle with sensitive digestion, chronic tear staining, and coat problems that no amount of grooming seems to fix. Raw feeding addresses these issues from the inside out.

Maltese Nutritional Needs

Adult Maltese typically weigh between 4 and 7 pounds, though healthy specimens often fall in the 5-6 pound range. Despite their small size, they're sturdy little dogs with surprisingly robust constitutions. The breed has ancient origins in the Mediterranean region, where they served as companion dogs for royalty and aristocracy.

Maltese have high metabolisms relative to their body size, much like other toy breeds. They burn energy quickly and need regular meals to maintain stable blood sugar and energy levels. Their small stomachs can only hold tiny amounts at a time, making nutrient density critical.

The breed's signature feature is their long, silky, pure white coat. Unlike most dogs with double coats, Maltese have a single coat of hair (not fur) that grows continuously like human hair. This means their coat requirements are more similar to human hair than typical dog fur. Nutrition plays an enormous role in coat quality, length, and manageability.

Maltese are notorious for having sensitive digestive systems. Many struggle with chronic digestive upset on commercial foods, experiencing gas, loose stools, vomiting, or picky eating. This sensitivity often improves dramatically on raw food, which is closer to what their ancestors evolved to eat.

Managing Sensitive Digestion

If you've owned a Maltese, you know their digestive systems can be challenging. Gas, soft stools, vomiting after eating, and finicky eating habits are common complaints. Raw feeding often provides relief when commercial foods fail.

The problem with many commercial foods is the processing itself. High heat processing denatures proteins, destroys enzymes, and creates advanced glycation end products (AGEs) that promote inflammation. The fillers, preservatives, and rendered proteins in kibble can irritate sensitive digestive tracts.

Raw food is easier to digest because it contains natural enzymes that aid breaking down food. The proteins remain in their natural state, making them less likely to trigger immune responses. Without the starches and fibers that bulk up kibble, raw meals are more concentrated and nutritious per bite.

When transitioning a sensitive Maltese, go slowly. Start with a single, gentle protein—turkey or rabbit work well as introduction proteins. Add a little to each meal while reducing kibble portion. After 1-2 weeks, you can increase the raw proportion. Some sensitive Maltese need 3-4 weeks for full transition.

Probiotics help during transitions and ongoing digestive health. A canine-specific probiotic provides beneficial bacteria that support digestion and immune function. Many owners continue probiotics long-term with good results.

Bone content affects stool quality. Too much bone creates chalky, hard stools. Too little bone can contribute to loose stools. Most Maltese do well with 7-10% bone content in their diet. Adjust based on what you observe.

Tear Stain Management

Those rust-colored stains under a Maltese's eyes are one of the breed's most frustrating cosmetic issues. Tear staining has multiple causes, and diet plays a significant role.

The primary cause of tear staining is porphyrin, an iron-containing compound excreted in tears, saliva, and urine. When porphyrin sits on white fur, it oxidizes to a reddish-brown color. High porphyrin levels lead to more staining. Some dogs naturally produce more porphyrin than others, but diet can influence the amount.

Yeast overgrowth contributes to staining. Maltese with compromised digestion often develop dysbiosis—an imbalance in gut bacteria that allows yeast to proliferate. This yeast (usually Malassezia) produces compounds that intensify staining. The sweet, musty odor some Maltese develop often indicates yeast issues.

Food allergies and sensitivities trigger inflammation that increases tear production and staining. Many commercial foods contain allergens like chicken, beef, dairy, wheat, and corn that trigger reactions in sensitive Maltese. Switching to a novel protein raw diet often reduces allergic responses and tear staining.

Raw feeding helps reduce tear staining by eliminating allergens, supporting healthy digestion, and providing nutrients that reduce inflammation. Some specific foods that help include:

Filtered water. Tap water with high mineral content or chlorine can worsen staining. Switching to filtered or distilled water reduces mineral intake that contributes to staining.

Fresh, novel proteins. Lamb, rabbit, duck, or venison eliminate common allergens. Rotate proteins to prevent new sensitivities from developing.

Probiotics. Support healthy gut flora and reduce yeast overgrowth. Many owners notice reduced staining within weeks of adding probiotics.

Coat Care Through Nutrition

The Maltese coat is hair, not fur. It grows continuously like human hair and requires constant nutrition to maintain its legendary silkiness. Many Maltese with dull, brittle, or thin coats improve dramatically on raw food.

Protein is the foundation of coat health. Hair is made of keratin, an animal protein. Without adequate high-quality protein, the coat becomes thin, brittle, and loses its shine. The amino acids in raw meat support hair growth from the follicle outward.

Omega-3 fatty acids from fish reduce inflammation in hair follicles and support coat shine. EPA and DHA also reduce skin inflammation that can cause itching and scratching, which damages the coat. Feed raw fish like sardines 2-3 times per week for optimal omega-3 intake.

Omega-6 fatty acids from animal fats contribute to coat softness and sheen. Most meats contain adequate omega-6, but the ratio between omega-3 and omega-6 matters. Modern diets often skew heavily toward omega-6, creating inflammation. Adding omega-3s helps restore balance.

Biotin supports coat condition. Egg yolks are excellent biotin sources and provide other beneficial nutrients. Adding a raw egg yolk to meals a few times per week boosts coat nutrition.

Zinc from organ meats supports skin cell turnover and coat pigment. Some Maltese with coat problems benefit from increased zinc intake, particularly organ meat lovers.

Daily Portion Guidelines

Maltese typically need 2-3% of body weight in raw food daily, depending on activity level and metabolism. A typical adult Maltese might weigh 5 pounds, requiring about 1.6 to 2.4 ounces of raw food per day.

These tiny portions mean precision matters. Invest in a kitchen scale that measures in ounces or grams. Eyeballing portions with a 5-pound dog invites weight management problems.

Body condition monitoring is important. You should be able to feel (but not see) ribs. There should be a visible waist when viewed from above. If ribs are prominent, add food. If the waist is disappearing, reduce portions.

Maltese puppies need significantly more food relative to size—typically 5-8% of body weight daily, split across three to four meals. Growing puppies need fuel for development. Never severely restrict puppy food.

Some adult Maltese benefit from three smaller meals rather than two larger ones. The smaller, more frequent meals help maintain stable blood sugar and reduce digestive stress. This is especially true for Maltese with sensitive stomachs or hypoglycemia concerns.

Best Foods for Maltese

Maltese with sensitive systems often do best with gentle, novel proteins. Starting with the right protein makes the difference between successful transition and digestive upset.

Turkey is often the best starting protein for sensitive Maltese. It's gentle on digestion and usually well-accepted. Ground turkey or turkey thighs work well.

Rabbit is extremely gentle and novel for most dogs. It's lean and usually doesn't trigger sensitivities. Rabbit can be harder to find but is worth seeking out for sensitive dogs.

Lamb is fattier than turkey, which some Maltese find appealing. It's a good rotation protein and works for many dogs with chicken sensitivities.

Duck provides variety and omega-3s. Duck feet are excellent for dental health and joint support. Duck is another good option for rotation.

Fish like sardines provide omega-3s for coat and skin health. Feed 2-3 times per week as part of rotation.

Organ meats should comprise about 10% of diet, with half being liver. Chicken liver, beef liver, and kidney provide variety in secreting organs.

Transitioning Your Maltese

Maltese can be picky eaters, so patience is essential during transition. Some take to raw immediately; others need weeks of coaxing.

Start with a small amount of a gentle protein—turkey or rabbit work well. Offer it as a treat between regular meals. Most Maltese can't resist meat, so this usually succeeds.

After a week of treat introduction, start replacing part of one meal with raw. Watch stool quality closely. Loose stools might indicate too much organ meat or a protein that doesn't agree. Hard, chalky stools indicate too much bone.

Temperature affects acceptance significantly. Room temperature food is more aromatic and appealing than cold food from the fridge. Warm food slightly if your Maltese seems reluctant.

If your Maltese refuses a particular protein, try another. Variety in proteins often helps—some dogs strongly prefer one protein over another. The goal is finding proteins that work for your individual dog.

Hand feeding helps build positive associations and lets you monitor exactly how much your Maltese eats. Many Maltese become enthusiastic raw eaters once they develop a taste for it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What causes tear staining in Maltese?

Tear staining has multiple causes. The primary staining compound is porphyrin, excreted in tears, saliva, and urine. When porphyrin oxidizes on white fur, it turns rust-colored. Yeast overgrowth, food allergies, eye irritation, blocked tear ducts, and mineral content in water can all worsen staining. Raw feeding helps by eliminating allergens, supporting digestion, and reducing inflammation. See our beginner's guide for more on sensitive dogs.

Can Maltese handle raw bones?

Most adult Maltese can handle appropriately sized raw bones. Their small mouths need small bones—quail bones, small chicken necks, or finely ground bone work well. Supervise bone chewing closely, especially initially. If your Maltese tends to gulp without chewing, stick with ground bone for safety.

How does raw feeding help sensitive Maltese stomachs?

Commercial foods contain allergens, preservatives, and rendered proteins that can irritate sensitive digestive systems. Raw food eliminates these triggers and provides natural enzymes that aid digestion. The proteins remain in their natural state, making them less likely to trigger immune responses. Many owners report reduced gas, firmer stools, and improved appetite after switching to raw.

What supplements help Maltese on raw diets?

Probiotics support digestive health and reduce yeast overgrowth. Fish oil or whole fish boosts omega-3s for coat health. For tear staining specifically, some owners use black soldier fly larvae (natural source of lauric acid that reduces porphyrin). Discuss supplements with your vet based on your Maltese's specific needs.

How much should I feed my Maltese puppy?

Maltese puppies need 5-8% of body weight daily, split across three to four meals. A 2-pound Maltese puppy needs about 1.6-2.6 ounces of food per day total. Growing puppies have enormous energy needs. Never severely restrict puppy food. Adjust portions based on growth rate and body condition.

My Maltese won't eat raw food. What should I do?

Try different proteins—some Maltese have strong preferences. Warm the food to enhance aroma. Add a splash of bone broth (no onion or garlic). Hand feed to build positive associations. You can also try mixing a small amount of raw with current food and gradually increasing the ratio over 2-3 weeks. Patience is key—some Maltese take longer to transition than others.

How long does it take to see coat improvements?

Coat improvements typically appear within 2-3 months of switching to raw. The new coat growth coming in will be stronger and shinier, gradually replacing older, damaged coat. Tear staining may improve within weeks if food allergies were the cause. Every dog is different, so give it a full 3-4 months before judging results.
