Prey Model Raw Diet for Small Breeds: Complete Guide

The Prey Model Raw (PMR) diet replicates what wolves and wild dogs consume when they catch and eat prey animals. It's the most ancestral approach to raw feeding, focusing on specific ratios of muscle meat, organs, and bone. For small breed dogs, PMR offers a straightforward framework that makes meal planning simple. This guide covers everything you need to implement PMR successfully for your small dog.

What Is the Prey Model?

The Prey Model is based on the premise that dogs evolved to eat whole prey animals. When a wild dog catches a rabbit or bird, they consume the entire animal: the muscle meat, organs, bones, and whatever small amounts of stomach contents might be present. PMR attempts to replicate this complete prey consumption through specific ratios.

The PMR Ratio:

The classic PMR ratio is 80% muscle meat, 10% bone, 5% liver, and 5% other secreting organs. This formulation reflects analyses of wild canid prey animals and attempts to recreate what a dog would consume from eating whole prey.

Muscle meat provides protein and fat. Bone provides calcium and phosphorus. Liver provides vitamins A and D, along with other nutrients. Secreting organs (kidney, spleen, brain, pancreas, etc.) provide additional vitamins, minerals, and micronutrients not found in muscle meat alone.

The Prey Model intentionally excludes plant matter. Wild dogs might ingest tiny amounts of plant material from prey stomachs, but no significant vegetable content exists in whole prey. PMR followers believe plant matter is unnecessary for canine nutrition and potentially inflammatory for some dogs.

PMR is the stricter of the two main raw feeding philosophies. Some dogs thrive on this straightforward approach. Others do better with the flexibility of BARF, which incorporates vegetables. Both approaches can work; the choice depends on your dog's individual needs. For an overview of the alternative approach, see our BARF Diet Guide.

PMR vs BARF: Understanding the Differences

If you've researched raw feeding, you've encountered both PMR and BARF. Understanding their differences helps you choose the right approach for your small dog.

PMR (Prey Model Raw)

PMR focuses on replicating whole prey through simple ratios: 80% muscle, 10% bone, 5% liver, 5% secreting organs. No plant matter is included. PMR followers believe this closest mimics ancestral canine diets and avoids potentially problematic plant ingredients.

The simplicity of PMR appeals to many feeders. The ratios are straightforward, meal planning is easy, and there's no need to source or prepare vegetables. Some dogs with digestive sensitivities do better without plant matter, which supports PMR for those individuals.

BARF (Biologically Appropriate Raw Food)

BARF includes approximately 70-80% muscle meat, 10% bone, 5-10% liver, 5% other organs, and 10-20% vegetables. The theory is that wild dogs and wolves get some plant material from prey stomachs, and vegetables provide fiber, antioxidants, and phytonutrients.

BARF allows more flexibility in meal planning and can be more cost-effective since vegetables are cheaper than meat. Some dogs do well with the additional fiber, which can firm up stools. For small dogs, the extra bulk might also help with satiety.

Which Approach Is Better?

Both approaches produce healthy dogs when properly implemented. The "right" choice depends on your dog. Dogs with plant sensitivities or digestive issues often do better on PMR. Dogs who seem to need more fiber or variety might thrive on BARF. Many experienced feeders try both and land somewhere between, using the frameworks as guides rather than rigid rules.

Adjusting PMR for Small Dogs

The PMR ratio works for dogs of all sizes, but practical implementation differs for small breeds.

Bone Content for Small Dogs

Small dogs need appropriately sized bones, which affects how we achieve the 10% bone ratio. Whole bone-in pieces (chicken necks, quail) naturally provide bone in the context of muscle meat. Ground bone mixed with boneless meat is an alternative that achieves the same ratio.

For toy breeds and very small dogs, ground bone might be necessary until teeth and chewing ability develop. As puppies grow or adults adjust, introduce whole bone pieces gradually. The goal is eventually to feed bone-in pieces that provide the 10% naturally.

Organ Ratios for Small Dogs

The 5% liver and 5% other organs might seem like tiny amounts for a 5-pound dog. But percentage applies to total food weight—a 5-pound dog eating about 50-75 grams daily needs approximately 4-7.5 grams of liver. That's about a teaspoon, which is measurable and important.

Weighing portions becomes more critical for small dogs. An error that represents a quarter-pound of food in a large dog represents a significant percentage error in a small dog. Invest in a small digital scale for accurate portioning.

Daily Versus Weekly Calculations

Some PMR feeders prefer to calculate ratios weekly rather than daily. Over a week, muscle meat averages 80%, bone 10%, liver 5%, and other organs 5%. This approach allows more flexibility in individual meals while maintaining overall ratios.

For a small dog eating 350 grams weekly (50 grams daily), weekly targets are: 280 grams muscle, 35 grams bone, 17.5 grams liver, 17.5 grams other organs. You could feed muscle-heavy meals some days and organ-heavy meals others, as long as weekly totals balance.

Whole Prey Options for Small Breeds

Whole prey is the most natural expression of PMR. A small prey animal eaten entirely provides perfect ratios without requiring meal assembly.

Day-Old Chicks

Day-old chicks are appropriately sized for very small dogs. They provide complete nutrition in a single item. Many small dogs enthusiastically consume day-old chicks. They're relatively inexpensive and can be ordered from poultry suppliers.

Day-old chicks have soft bones and are easy to digest. They're an excellent option for toy breeds or as occasional variety. Feed frozen (thawed) for safety. One or two chicks might constitute a full meal for a small dog.

Quail

Whole quail is perfect for small to medium small dogs. A whole quail provides muscle, bone, organs, and all the nutrition a dog needs in appropriate ratios. Quail is available from specialty butchers, game bird suppliers, and some grocery stores.

Quail sizes vary. Choose quail your dog can consume in one or two sittings. Small quail might be eaten entirely; larger quail might provide two meals. The bone in quail is small and soft, suitable for small jaws.

Rabbits

Small rabbits provide multiple meals for small dogs. Whole small rabbits can be fed in portions or entirely for larger small breeds. Rabbit is lean, highly digestible, and typically well-tolerated by dogs with protein sensitivities.

Commercially available whole prey rabbits are often sorted by size. Choose appropriately sized rabbits for your dog. A small rabbit might be ideal for a 10-15 pound dog; larger rabbits might work for dogs over 15 pounds.

Mice and Rats

Small frozen feeder mice and rats are available from reptile food suppliers. These provide complete nutrition in tiny packages. Mice might be appropriate meals for very small dogs or treats for larger small breeds.

Mice and rats are nutritionally complete but may carry higher pathogen risk than poultry-based options. Source from reputable suppliers who raise rodents specifically for consumption. Thaw thoroughly and inspect for quality before feeding.

Building Complete PMR Meals

When not feeding whole prey, you'll assemble PMR meals from individual components. Here's how to build complete meals.

The 80/10/5/5 Framework

Every complete PMR meal (or daily/weekly total) should contain approximately 80% muscle meat, 10% raw meaty bone, 5% liver, and 5% other secreting organs.

Muscle meat includes any skeletal muscle from any animal. Heart counts as muscle meat (not as a secreting organ, despite being an organ). Choose various proteins for variety.

Raw meaty bone means bone with muscle attached, which naturally provides the right ratio. Chicken necks, wings, and frames work well. Alternatively, add ground bone to boneless meat.

Liver is liver—any species works. Liver is nutrient-dense and should be limited to 5% to avoid vitamin A toxicity.

Other secreting organs include kidney, spleen, brain, pancreas, testicles, thymus, and others. These are sometimes called "secondary organs" and provide nutrients not abundant in liver.

Example Meal Calculation

For a 10-pound dog eating 100 grams daily:

  • Muscle meat: 80 grams (could be 70g chicken thigh + 10g beef heart)
  • Bone: 10 grams (from bone-in chicken, or 10g ground bone added)
  • Liver: 5 grams (chicken liver, beef liver, etc.)
  • Other organs: 5 grams (kidney, spleen, etc.)

Sample Protein Rotation

Rotate through different animal sources over weeks or months. This provides diverse amino acid profiles and micronutrients. A simple rotation might include: chicken (2-3 days), beef (1-2 days), turkey (1-2 days), fish (1 day), rabbit (1 day). Adjust based on availability and your dog's preferences.

Practical Weekly Meal Plan

Here's a sample weekly PMR plan for a 12-pound small dog eating approximately 120 grams daily (840 grams weekly).

Weekly Targets:

  • Muscle meat: 672 grams (80%)
  • Bone: 84 grams (10%)
  • Liver: 42 grams (5%)
  • Other organs: 42 grams (5%)

Day 1 (Chicken)

Morning: 60g chicken thigh with bone
Evening: 60g chicken with bone, 6g chicken liver, 3g kidney, 3g spleen

Day 2 (Chicken)

Morning: 60g chicken frame with meat
Evening: 60g chicken neck, 6g chicken liver, 3g kidney, 3g beef heart

Day 3 (Beef)

Morning: 60g beef with bone (small pieces)
Evening: 60g beef heart, 6g beef liver, 6g kidney

Day 4 (Turkey)

Morning: 60g turkey neck
Evening: 60g turkey thigh with bone, 6g chicken liver, 6g turkey kidney

Day 5 (Rabbit)

Morning: 60g rabbit with bone
Evening: 60g rabbit, 6g rabbit liver, 6g kidney

Day 6 (Fish)

Morning: 60g sardines (whole, with bone)
Evening: 60g salmon, 6g beef liver, 6g kidney

Day 7 (Beef/Turkey)

Morning: 60g beef
Evening: 60g turkey, 6g chicken liver, 6g kidney

Adjust portion sizes based on your individual dog's weight, activity level, and body condition. Some days might be heavier or lighter depending on bone content of specific cuts. This is normal—what matters is maintaining weekly targets.

Troubleshooting PMR Issues

Even well-planned PMR diets sometimes need adjustment. Here are common issues and solutions.

Loose Stools

Loose stools on PMR usually indicate insufficient bone content. Add more bone (5% more) and monitor. If that doesn't help, the issue might be too much organ meat—reduce liver and other organs slightly. Very soft stools might indicate food quality issues or rapid diet changes.

Constipation or Chalky Stools

Constipation or white, chalky, crumbly stools indicate too much bone. Reduce bone content by 5% and ensure adequate hydration. Some dogs produce chalky stools when transitioning; this usually resolves as digestion adapts. Persistent chalky stools require bone reduction.

Dogs Who Won't Eat Organs

Some dogs pick out muscle meat and leave organs. This creates nutritional imbalances over time. Solutions include grinding organs with muscle, hiding organ pieces inside muscle meat balls, or gradually transitioning to accepting organs through persistence. Most dogs eventually accept organs when hungry enough.

Boredom with Protein

Feeding the same protein repeatedly leads to boredom and potential new allergies. Rotate through at least 3-5 proteins over time. Some dogs need more frequent rotation to stay interested. For help with variety, see our allergies guide.

Weight Management Issues

If your small dog gains weight on PMR, reduce portions slightly. If they lose weight or seem perpetually hungry, increase portions. Remember that muscle meat can vary in fat content—leaner cuts might require larger portions for adequate calories. Adjust based on body condition, not arbitrary formulas.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between PMR and BARF?

PMR (Prey Model Raw) uses 80/10/5/5 ratios (muscle/bone/liver/other organs) with no plant matter. BARF adds 10-20% vegetables to the mix. Both can produce healthy dogs; the choice depends on your individual dog. Some dogs with plant sensitivities do better on PMR. For more on BARF, see our BARF guide.

Can small dogs get enough calcium from PMR?

Yes, when bone content is appropriate (10% of diet). Small dogs eating bone-in pieces (chicken necks, quail) or appropriately added ground bone get sufficient calcium. Very small dogs might need ground bone until they can handle whole bones. Monitor stool quality—firm stools indicate adequate bone.

What counts as a "secreting organ"?

Secreting organs include kidney, spleen, brain, pancreas, thymus, thyroid, adrenal glands, testicles, and ovaries. These glands produce and secrete substances, hence the name. Heart is a muscle organ, not a secreting organ, and counts toward the 80% muscle category.

Do I need to feed whole prey?

No, whole prey is convenient but not required. PMR can be achieved by combining muscle meat, bone, liver, and other organs in proper ratios. Many feeders use whole prey occasionally for variety while primarily feeding assembled meals. Either approach works as long as ratios are maintained.

Can I calculate PMR ratios daily or only weekly?

Either approach works. Some feeders calculate daily (ensuring each meal has proper ratios); others balance weekly (some meals heavier in organs, others in muscle, with weekly totals correct). Daily calculation provides more consistency; weekly calculation offers more flexibility. Choose what works for your lifestyle.

What if my dog refuses to eat organs?

Many dogs initially avoid organs due to strong flavor. Solutions: grind organs with muscle meat, hide small organ pieces inside meat balls, try different organ types (kidney vs spleen have different flavors), or simply persist through hunger. Most dogs eventually accept organs. Essential organs for health are liver and at least one other secreting organ.

How do I know if my PMR diet is working?

Healthy indicators include: firm, small stools; healthy coat and skin; normal energy levels; stable weight; normal digestion. Many owners notice improved coat shine, better breath, reduced stool volume, and more consistent energy within weeks. Regular vet checkups confirm overall health.

Should I add supplements to PMR?

A properly formulated PMR diet is complete without supplements for most dogs. Fish oil can benefit coat and joint health and is easy to add. If feeding a limited protein rotation, a broad-spectrum supplement might be appropriate. For details, see our supplements guide.
