Havanese dogs are living sunshine—cheerful, affectionate, and draped in a luxurious silky coat that flows like a tropical breeze. These Cuban companions have captured hearts worldwide with their playful personalities and stunning good looks. Keeping a Havanese healthy and happy requires understanding their unique nutritional needs, particularly when it comes to supporting that magnificent coat and maintaining their cheerful digestion.

Understanding Havanese Nutrition

Havanese typically weigh between 7 and 13 pounds, making them solidly in the toy breed category. Despite their small size, they're surprisingly sturdy and athletic. The breed standard describes a dog with a springy gait and an alert, curious expression—these are not delicate lap dogs but active companions who enjoy being involved in everything their families do.

The breed originated in Cuba, where they were cherished companions of Cuban aristocracy. Their ancestors include the Tenerife dog and other European toy breeds brought to Cuba by settlers. This history of being treasured companions shaped a breed that thrives on attention and forms deep bonds with their families.

Havanese are known for having relatively sensitive digestive systems. Many struggle with food sensitivities, particularly to common proteins like chicken and beef. They're also prone to allergies that manifest through skin and coat problems. These digestive sensitivities often improve dramatically on raw diets, which eliminate the problematic fillers and allergens in commercial foods.

The breed's long, silky coat is one of its most striking features, and nutrition plays a crucial role in maintaining coat quality. A dull, dry, brittle coat often signals nutritional deficiencies or food sensitivities. Switching to raw food typically produces remarkable improvements in coat shine and texture within weeks. Coat Health Through Diet

The Havanese coat is a single layer of silky hair (unlike double-coated breeds) that continuously grows rather than shedding seasonally. This coat type requires excellent nutrition to maintain its luxurious appearance. What you feed directly impacts how your Havanese looks.

Omega-3 fatty acids are the foundation of coat health. DHA and EPA from fish oils support skin cell health, reduce inflammation, and create the soft, shiny coat Havanese are known for. Without adequate omega-3s, coats become dry, dull, and prone to breakage. Feed fatty fish 2-3 times weekly, or add a quality fish oil supplement to your Havanese's meals.

Zinc from organ meats and certain protein sources supports skin health and coat quality. Beef and lamb are particularly good zinc sources. Havanese with coat problems often improve when zinc is added to their diet through raw organ content. Liver provides zinc alongside its many other nutrients.

Vitamin A supports skin cell turnover and coat quality. The vitamin A in liver is highly bioavailable. However, excessive vitamin A causes toxicity, so liver should remain limited to 5% of total food intake. A small amount of liver provides significant benefits without risk.

Biotin and B vitamins support overall coat health. These are found in organ meats, muscle meat, and some vegetables. A varied raw diet provides adequate B vitamins for most Havanese. If coat problems persist despite good nutrition, consult your vet to rule out underlying health conditions.

Protein quality matters for coat growth. Hair is made of keratin, a protein, so adequate high-quality protein is essential. The amino acids in raw meat support keratin production better than plant proteins. Aim for protein content that matches or exceeds what your Havanese would get from quality commercial foods.

Managing Sensitive Digestion

Havanese frequently have sensitive digestive systems that react to dietary changes or problematic ingredients. Many improve significantly on raw food, but the transition needs careful management to avoid exacerbating digestive issues.

Start with novel proteins if your Havanese shows sensitivity signs. Chicken and beef are common triggers, so consider starting with turkey, rabbit, or duck instead. Novel proteins haven't been encountered by your dog's immune system, so they provoke fewer reactions. Some Havanese do well on exotic proteins like venison or kangaroo.

Introduce new foods slowly. For the first week, offer just a few bites of the new protein alongside familiar food. If digestion remains stable, gradually increase the new protein over 2-3 weeks. This slow approach lets you identify triggers before they cause significant problems.

Organ content requires attention for sensitive Havanese. Organ meats are rich and can cause loose stools if introduced too quickly. Start with muscle meat only for the first week. Then add 5% liver. After another week, add 5% other secreting organs. This gradual approach helps sensitive systems adjust.

Bone content affects stool quality significantly. Too much bone causes constipation; too little causes loose stools. Start with bone content around 8-10% and adjust based on what you see. Firm, small stools indicate good bone content. White, chalky stools suggest too much bone. Loose stools may indicate too little.

Probiotics support digestive health. Some Havanese benefit from added probiotics, especially during transitions. Small amounts of fermented foods like plain kefir or finely minced raw fermented vegetables can help establish healthy gut flora. Start with tiny amounts to avoid digestive upset.

Daily Portion Guide

Adult Havanese typically need 2-2.5% of body weight daily, with adjustments based on activity level and individual metabolism.

A typical 10-pound adult Havanese needs approximately 100-125 grams (3.5-4.4 ounces) of raw food daily, split into two meals. More active dogs may need closer to 3%, while less active or senior dogs may do well on 2%. Watch body condition to guide adjustments.

Havanese puppies have significant nutritional needs for growth. A growing Havanese needs 5-8% of body weight daily, split across three to four meals. Young puppies under four months often need four meals daily to prevent hypoglycemia and support their rapid growth. Never restrict puppy food to avoid overfeeding—puppies need abundant nutrition.

Monitor body condition weekly. You should be able to feel (but not see) your Havanese's ribs. There should be a visible waist when viewed from above and a tucked abdomen from the side. A dull, dry coat can signal inadequate nutrition even at proper weight. Changes in stool quality often indicate portion adjustments are needed.

Pregnant and nursing Havanese have dramatically increased needs. During the last trimester, gradually increase portions to 125-150% of normal intake. Lactating mothers often need 200-300% of normal portions, fed in frequent small meals throughout the day. The demands of nursing are extreme—mother Havanese can quickly become malnourished without careful feeding.

Ideal Protein Selection

Havanese often do best with varied protein sources, which provides diverse nutrients and reduces sensitivity risks.

Turkey is an excellent starter protein for many Havanese. It's usually well-dolerated and provides good nutrition. Ground turkey is easy to portion for small meals. Turkey hearts are small, affordable, and nutrient-dense. Turkey necks provide bone content. Many Havanese love dark turkey meat's strong flavor.

Rabbit is one of the best proteins for sensitive Havanese. It's a novel protein for most dogs, meaning fewer sensitivity reactions. Rabbit is lean and highly digestible. Whole prey rabbit (small enough to eat entirely) provides complete nutrition in an ideal form. Many owners report coat improvements when switching to rabbit.

Lamb is often well-tolerated by dogs who react to chicken or beef. It's moderately fatty and provides good nutrition. Lamb hearts are affordable organ options. Ground lamb works well for meal preparation. Some Havanese prefer lamb's stronger flavor to milder poultry.

Fish should be a regular part of rotation for Havanese. The omega-3s benefit coat and skin dramatically. Sardines are one of the best options—they're small, boneless, and incredibly nutritious. Salmon, mackerel, and herring also provide excellent omega-3 profiles. Feed fish 1-2 times weekly.

Beef provides variety and iron. It's often tolerated by Havanese who react to poultry. Beef heart counts as muscle meat and provides taurine and CoQ10. Use beef as part of rotation rather than a sole protein source. Some Havanese are more sensitive to beef; watch for any adverse reactions.

Weight Management

Havanese are prone to obesity, which affects their health and longevity. The breed's small size means weight gain happens easily, and even a pound or two of excess weight matters significantly on a 10-pound frame.

Raw feeding supports weight management because protein and fat satisfy hunger more effectively than carbohydrates. Most owners find their Havanese eat less by volume while appearing equally satisfied. The moisture content of raw food contributes to feelings of fullness.

Portions should be calculated based on ideal (not current) body weight for dogs who need to lose weight. Start with 2% of current weight and monitor. If your Havanese seems constantly hungry, the transition may need adjustment, or you may need to increase portions slightly. Gradual weight loss of 1-2% of body weight weekly is healthy.

Exercise supports weight management and overall health. Havanese enjoy walks, play sessions, and interactive games. Regular activity maintains muscle tone and prevents boredom. Even small amounts of daily exercise make a difference—two 15-minute walks plus play time is usually sufficient for adult Havanese.

Hidden calories from treats derail weight management. Track everything your Havanese eats, including training treats and food offered during cooking. Small pieces of meat make better treats than commercial treats with added calories. A few extra treats daily can prevent weight loss or even cause weight gain.

Transition Tips

Transitioning a Havanese to raw food requires patience, especially given the breed's sensitive digestion. A gradual approach reduces digestive upset and lets you identify any problematic ingredients.

Week one focuses on introduction. Offer tiny amounts of raw food as treats between normal meals. Choose a novel protein if your Havanese has shown sensitivity signs. This approach builds positive associations without overwhelming the digestive system.

Week two involves replacing one meal partially. If week one went smoothly, start replacing about half of one meal with raw. Watch for any digestive upset, changes in stool quality, or behavioral changes. Some loose stools are normal during this phase.

Week three moves toward full transition. Replace one complete meal with raw food. Most Havanese accept this change smoothly with a gradual approach. Fine-tune organ content and bone ratios based on stool quality.

Week four and beyond is full raw feeding territory. Your Havanese should be thriving. Continue rotating proteins for variety. Monitor weight monthly and adjust portions as needed. If coat quality or digestion seems off, look for dietary causes. For additional transition strategies, see our detailed transition guide.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does raw feeding improve Havanese coat health?

Raw feeding provides bioavailable omega-3 fatty acids from fish, which support skin cell health and create shiny, soft coats. The zinc and vitamin A from organ meats support coat growth. Protein quality improves keratin production. Without the inflammatory ingredients in processed foods, skin irritation decreases. Most owners notice significant coat improvements within 4-8 weeks.

My Havanese has a sensitive stomach. What proteins should I try?

Start with novel proteins like turkey, rabbit, or duck. These proteins haven't triggered immune responses in most dogs. Avoid chicken and beef initially if your Havanese has shown sensitivity. Introduce new proteins slowly, one at a time, waiting a week between rotations. If sensitivities persist, try exotic proteins like venison or kangaroo.

Can Havanese eat raw bones safely?

Yes, when sized appropriately. Havanese can handle small chicken necks, wing tips, and quail. Always supervise bone chewing. If your Havanese tends to gulp rather than chew, use ground bone instead. Raw bones are soft and safe; never feed cooked bones. Start with smaller, softer bones if your Havanese hasn't eaten bones before.

How much should I feed my Havanese?

Most adult Havanese need 2-2.5% of body weight daily. A 10-pound Havanese typically needs 100-125 grams (3.5-4.4 ounces) split into two meals. Adjust based on activity level, age, and body condition. You should feel but not see ribs. Puppies need 5-8% of body weight daily. Check our portion calculator for personalized recommendations.

What supplements does my Havanese need on a raw diet?

A varied raw diet with proper organ content usually provides everything most Havanese need. Fish oil can benefit coat health if fish isn't fed regularly. Probiotics may help dogs with sensitive digestion. For specific recommendations, consult your veterinarian based on your individual dog's health status.

How do I transition my Havanese to raw food?

Take 2-3 weeks for a gradual transition. Start with tiny amounts of raw as treats. Replace one meal partially in week two. Replace one complete meal in week three. If your Havanese has sensitive digestion, go even slower. Watch stool quality and adjust ratios. Patience is key—don't rush the process.

Are Havanese prone to food allergies?

Havanese can develop food sensitivities like any breed. Chicken and beef are common triggers. Signs include itchy skin, ear infections, digestive upset, and coat problems. If you suspect allergies, try an elimination diet with a novel protein. See our allergies guide for detailed elimination diet protocols.
