Getting raw food portions right is one of the biggest concerns for new raw feeders. Feed too little and your small dog loses weight, energy, and muscle. Feed too much and they pack on pounds that stress their tiny frames. This guide walks you through calculating exact portions for your small dog.

How to Calculate Raw Food Portions

Raw feeding portions depend on several factors: body weight, age, activity level, metabolism, and reproductive status. No single formula works for every dog, but the body weight percentage method provides an excellent starting point that you can adjust based on results.

The goal is to maintain lean body condition while providing adequate nutrition for energy, muscle maintenance, and overall health. For most adult small dogs, this falls between 2-3% of body weight daily, with the exact percentage varying based on individual factors.

Here's the basic formula:

Daily food amount (ounces) = Body weight (pounds) × Percentage × 16

For example, a 10-pound dog eating 2.5% of body weight: 10 × 0.025 × 16 = 4 ounces daily

Let's break this down step by step.

Body Weight Percentage Method

The body weight percentage method is the standard approach for raw feeding calculations. The percentage represents what portion of your dog's body weight they'll eat in food daily.

Typical percentages by dog type:

  • 1.5-2%: Senior dogs, very inactive dogs, dogs needing weight loss
  • 2-2.5%: Typical adult maintenance, average activity
  • 2.5-3%: Active dogs, working dogs, high metabolism
  • 3-4%: Very active dogs, extremely high metabolism breeds

For small breeds specifically, many fall into the higher metabolism category. Toy breeds often need closer to 2.5-3% to maintain weight, while less active small breeds might do well on 2-2.5%.

Start with a conservative percentage (2-2.5% for most adults) and adjust based on body condition and energy levels over 2-4 weeks. It's easier to add food than to deal with weight you've let accumulate.

Activity Level Adjustments

Activity level significantly affects how much food your small dog needs. An active, playful Yorkie needs more than a couch potato Pomeranian of the same weight.

Sedentary (couch potato): Reduce to 1.5-2% of body weight. Many small dogs become less active as they age, and their portions need to follow suit. Watch for weight gain and reduce portions at the first sign.

Moderately active (daily walks, some play): 2-2.5% is usually appropriate. This covers most pet small dogs who get regular exercise but aren't competing in sports.

Very active (multiple walks, hiking, agility): Increase to 2.5-3%. Working small dogs, agility competitors, and extremely playful breeds may need the higher end of this range.

Highly athletic: Some small dogs involved in intensive sports may need 3-4% during training seasons. Discuss with a vet or canine nutritionist if your small dog is in intensive athletic training.

Season affects activity too. Many dogs are more active in warm months and less active when it's cold. Adjust portions seasonally if your dog's activity level changes throughout the year.

Illness, injury, and recovery periods also affect needs. Dogs recovering from illness or surgery often need more calories to rebuild strength. Senior dogs with arthritis may become less active and need portion reduction.

Puppy vs. Adult Portions

Puppies have vastly different nutritional needs than adults. Growing bodies require more food relative to size to support development.

Puppy percentages:

  • Under 4 months: 8-10% of body weight daily
  • 4-6 months: 6-8% of body weight daily
  • 6-12 months: 4-6% of body weight daily

Puppies should eat 3-4 meals daily until 6 months, then 2-3 meals. Their small stomachs can't hold enough food in 2 meals to support proper growth, especially for toy breeds prone to hypoglycemia.

Never restrict puppy food severely. Underfeeding during growth periods can lead to developmental problems, weak bones, and poor muscle development. Better to have a slightly chunky puppy than a stunted one.

As puppies approach adult size (typically 9-12 months for small breeds, though some continue growing until 14-16 months), gradually reduce portions to transition to adult percentages. Watch body condition and adjust as needed.

Pregnant and nursing dogs have dramatically increased needs. During late pregnancy, increase to 3-4% of body weight. Nursing mothers may need 4-8% or more depending on litter size and nursing intensity. Free-feeding (keeping food available at all times) is often appropriate for nursing mothers.

Breed-Specific Guidelines

Different small breeds have different metabolic rates and tendencies that affect portion needs.

Chihuahuas typically need 2.5-3% due to high metabolisms. Watch for hypoglycemia with smaller portions. Check our Chihuahua guide for detailed breed-specific advice.

Yorkies often do well on 2-2.5%, but active individuals may need more. Growing puppies need significantly more. See our Yorkshire Terrier guide.

French Bulldogs tend toward obesity and usually do best on 2-2.5%. Some less active individuals may need as low as 1.5-2%. Our Frenchie guide has detailed recommendations.

Dachshunds gain weight easily—start conservatively at 2% and adjust based on body condition. The breed's back issues make weight control especially important. See our Dachshund guide.

Pomeranians have high energy levels and usually need 2.5-3%. Watch weight carefully—fluffy coats hide weight gain. Our Pomeranian guide covers breed-specific needs.

Maltese typically do well on 2-2.5%. Sensitive stomachs mean gradual adjustments are important. Our Maltese guide provides detailed information.

Shih Tzus are prone to weight gain and usually need 2-2.5%. Less active individuals may need slightly less. See our Shih Tzu guide.

Let's walk through some example calculations to make the math clear.

Example 1: Adult Chihuahua

Weight: 4.5 lbs
Age: 3 years
Activity: Moderately active
Calculation: 4.5 × 0.025 × 16 = 1.8 oz daily
Split into 2 meals: 0.9 oz per meal

Example 2: French Bulldog needing weight loss

Weight: 22 lbs
Age: 5 years
Activity: Sedentary
Goal: Weight loss
Calculation: 22 × 0.02 × 16 = 7.0 oz daily
Split into 2 meals: 3.5 oz per meal

Example 3: Yorkie puppy

Current weight: 3 lbs
Age: 4 months
Activity: Very active (puppy energy!)
Calculation: 3 × 0.08 × 16 = 3.8 oz daily
Split into 3 meals: 1.3 oz per meal

Example 4: Active Dachshund

Weight: 12 lbs
Age: 4 years
Activity: Very active (hikes with owner)
Calculation: 12 × 0.03 × 16 = 5.8 oz daily
Split into 2 meals: 2.9 oz per meal

Example 5: Senior Maltese

Weight: 6 lbs
Age: 11 years
Activity: Sedentary
Calculation: 6 × 0.02 × 16 = 1.9 oz daily
Split into 2 meals: 1.0 oz per meal

Monitoring and Adjusting

Calculated portions are starting points. You must monitor your individual dog and adjust based on results.

Body condition scoring:

  • Too thin: Ribs, spine, and hip bones visible or easily felt with no fat cover. Waist extremely pronounced. Needs more food.
  • Ideal: Ribs easily felt but not seen. Waist visible when viewed from above. Belly tucked when viewed from side.
  • Overweight: Ribs difficult to feel under fat layer. Waist not visible. Belly may sag. Needs portion reduction.

Weigh your small dog monthly during the adjustment period, then quarterly once portions are stable. Small weight changes that you'd ignore in a large dog matter significantly for a 5-pound dog.

Energy levels: Your dog's energy provides clues about whether portions are appropriate. Good energy (alert, playful, able to exercise) suggests adequate nutrition. Low energy could mean insufficient portions or other health issues.

Stool quality: Proper raw feeding produces firm, easy-to-pick-up stools that aren't chalky or hard. Loose stools may indicate too much organ meat or need for more bone. Diarrhea warrants veterinary attention.

Changes in life circumstances require portion adjustments. If your dog becomes less active due to injury or aging, reduce portions. If they start intensive training or become much more active, increase portions. Breed spay/neuter status affects metabolism—intact dogs often need slightly more food than neutered dogs.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I weigh my small dog accurately?

For tiny dogs, baby scales work well—they measure in ounces and can accommodate your dog sitting or standing. You can also weigh yourself holding your dog, then weigh yourself alone, and subtract. Most pet stores carry baby scales or small bathroom scales suitable for small dogs. Accuracy matters because small errors represent large percentages of total food intake.

Should I feed my small dog once or twice daily?

Twice daily works for most adult small dogs. Some benefit from three smaller meals, especially toy breeds prone to hypoglycemia. Puppies under 6 months should eat 3-4 times daily. Regular schedules help regulate digestion and blood sugar. Free-feeding is generally not recommended because it makes monitoring appetite and weight difficult.

What if my dog seems hungry on the calculated portions?

First, verify that portions are actually adequate by checking body condition. A dog at ideal weight who's "hungry" between meals is normal—protein and fat satisfy differently than carbohydrates. Make sure bone content is adequate (more bone = more fullness). If your dog is underweight and hungry, increase portions. Some dogs with very high metabolisms genuinely need more food than formulas suggest.

How do I adjust portions during transition to raw?

During transition, feed smaller portions while your dog's digestion adapts. Start at 50-75% of target amounts and increase over 1-2 weeks. This prevents digestive upset while your dog's gut bacteria populations adjust. Once fully transitioned, settle on portions based on body condition.

Do treats count toward daily portions?

Yes, treats count toward total daily calories. For small dogs, even a few extra treats represent significant calorie additions. If you're training heavily and using lots of treats, reduce meal portions slightly to compensate. Use meat-based treats rather than carb-heavy commercial treats when possible.

My dog is gaining weight on the calculated portions. What should I do?

Reduce portions by 10-20% initially. For example, if feeding 4 ounces daily, drop to 3.2-3.6 ounces. Wait 2-3 weeks and reassess body condition. Continue reducing until weight stabilizes at ideal condition. For stubborn weight gain, also examine treat intake and activity level. Some small dogs need surprisingly little food to maintain weight.

How do seasonal changes affect portions?

Dogs are often more active in warmer months and less active when cold. Some dogs naturally eat more in winter to maintain body heat, while others are less active and need less food. Watch for seasonal weight fluctuations and adjust portions accordingly. Indoor dogs with consistent year-round activity may not need seasonal adjustments.
