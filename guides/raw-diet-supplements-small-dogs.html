Essential Supplements for Small Dogs on Raw Diet

One of the beautiful things about feeding raw is how complete and bioavailable the nutrition already is. A properly formulated raw diet provides most of what your small dog needs. But certain supplements fill important gaps and provide targeted support for breed-specific concerns. This guide helps you understand which supplements genuinely matter for small dogs on raw diets—and which ones are just expensive urine.

Why Supplements Matter on Raw Diets

A complete raw diet includes muscle meat, bone, liver, and other organ meats in roughly appropriate proportions. For most dogs eating a varied raw diet, supplements aren't strictly necessary. But "not strictly necessary" doesn't mean "useless." Strategic supplementation can address specific concerns and optimize health in ways food alone might not.

Small dogs have particular vulnerabilities that targeted supplements can address. Toy breeds face hypoglycemia risk that some supplements help prevent. Brachycephalic breeds benefit from anti-inflammatory support. Breeds prone to heart disease need cardiac support nutrients. Long-coated breeds benefit from supplements that enhance coat quality beyond what food provides.

The quality of raw ingredients affects supplementation needs. Meat from pasture-raised animals contains more omega-3s than grain-fed animal products. Organ meats from healthy animals provide more nutrients than organs from factory-farmed sources. If your budget or location limits access to high-quality ingredients, supplements help fill the gaps.

Individual variation matters too. Some dogs absorb certain nutrients less efficiently due to genetics or health conditions. Senior dogs often need more support than young adults. Dogs recovering from illness or injury have increased nutritional needs. Watch your individual dog to determine what supplementation serves them best.

Essential Supplements

These supplements provide genuine benefits for most small dogs on raw diets.

Fish Oil (Omega-3 Fatty Acids)

Fish oil is the supplement I recommend for nearly every raw-fed dog. The omega-3 fatty acids EPA and DHA support skin health, coat quality, joint comfort, heart function, and brain health. They're also anti-inflammatory, which benefits dogs with allergies, arthritis, or chronic inflammation.

Small dogs typically need about 300mg of combined EPA/DHA per 10 pounds of body weight weekly. This can come from whole fish meals (sardines, mackerel, salmon) fed 1-2 times weekly, or from concentrated fish oil supplements. Sardines provide this amount easily—just two small sardines per week for a 10-pound dog.

Choose fish oil supplements without added flavorings or sweeteners. Human-grade fish oil is usually better than pet-specific products, which often contain lower-quality oils. Store fish oil in the refrigerator to prevent rancidity. If your fish oil smells extremely fishy or has turned, it's oxidized and should be discarded.

Vitamin E

Vitamin E works synergistically with omega-3 fatty acids and protects cell membranes from oxidative damage. When feeding polyunsaturated fats (including fish oils), vitamin E becomes more important because these fats increase the body's need for antioxidant protection.

The typical dose is 2-3 IU per pound of body weight. A 10-pound dog needs about 20-30 IU of vitamin E daily. Most quality fish oil supplements contain vitamin E already, so check labels before adding separate vitamin E to avoid over-supplementation.

Natural vitamin E (d-alpha-tocopherol) is better absorbed than synthetic forms (dl-alpha-tocopherol). Look for supplements containing natural vitamin E. The selenium in raw meat provides additional antioxidant support, so excessive vitamin E isn't necessary.

Probiotics

Probiotics support healthy gut flora, which affects everything from digestion to immune function to skin health. Some dogs benefit from probiotic supplementation, especially during transitions to raw, after antibiotic treatment, or during times of stress.

Instead of expensive probiotic capsules, try adding small amounts of fermented foods to your dog's diet. Plain kefir (unsweetened, no flavorings) provides probiotics along with protein and fat. Finely minced raw fermented vegetables like sauerkraut offer similar benefits. Start with tiny amounts—a teaspoon for small dogs—to avoid digestive upset.

If using commercial probiotics, choose products specifically formulated for dogs. Human probiotics aren't harmful but may not colonize the canine gut as effectively. Look for products containing multiple bacterial strains, including Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species.

Kelp or Seaweed Powder

Kelp provides iodine and trace minerals that might be lacking in meat-focused raw diets. Small amounts of kelp powder (about 1/4 teaspoon for small dogs) provide minerals including iodine, zinc, iron, and selenium.

Not all dogs tolerate kelp well. Some develop digestive upset or thyroid issues from excessive iodine. Use kelp as an occasional supplement rather than a daily addition, especially for dogs with thyroid conditions. Moderation is key.

Breed-Specific Supplement Needs

Different breeds have different vulnerabilities. Here's how to tailor supplementation to your small dog's breed-specific concerns.

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels and Other Heart-Prone Breeds

Cavaliers are nearly all affected by mitral valve disease by age 10. While supplements won't prevent this genetic condition, certain nutrients support heart health and may slow disease progression.

Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10) supports energy production in heart cells. The typical dose for small dogs is 30-100mg daily. Ubiquinol (the reduced form) absorbs better than ubiquinone. Start supplementation around age 5-6, or earlier if MVD has been diagnosed.

Taurine is an amino acid crucial for heart function. While dogs can synthesize taurine, some breeds may benefit from dietary sources. Red meat and beef heart contain taurine naturally. Supplementation may benefit dogs with existing heart concerns. Discuss with your veterinarian before starting.

Omega-3s from fish oil support heart rhythm and reduce inflammation. This is one of the most evidence-supported supplements for cardiac health. The general fish oil recommendation applies—aim for 300mg EPA/DHA per 10 pounds of body weight weekly.

Brachycephalic Breeds (French Bulldogs, Boston Terriers, Pugs)

Flat-faced breeds struggle with breathing, which creates systemic stress and inflammation. Anti-inflammatory support through omega-3s helps reduce airway inflammation. Fish oil should be a staple for these breeds.

Quercetin, a natural flavonoid found in apples and onions (but available as a supplement), has antihistamine and anti-inflammatory properties. Some owners use it for dogs with allergic symptoms that affect breathing. Standard dose is about 100-200mg for small dogs daily.

Maintaining lean body weight is crucial for brachycephalic dogs. The most important "supplement" for these breeds is portion control to prevent obesity, which worsens breathing problems significantly.

Small Breeds Prone to Dental Disease

Most small breeds struggle with dental disease regardless of diet. While raw meaty bones provide dental benefits, some dogs need additional support.

Dental-specific probiotics may help reduce oral bacteria. Look for probiotic strains specifically marketed for dental health. Regular chewing on appropriate raw bones remains the most effective natural dental care.

Long-Coated Breeds (Maltese, Shih Tzu, Havanese, Yorkshire Terriers)

Breeds with continuously growing coats need extra support for skin and hair health. Omega-3s are the foundation. Biotin and zinc from organ meats support keratin production. Adding liver to the diet provides vitamin A that supports skin cell turnover.

If coat quality remains poor despite adequate omega-3s, consider a skin and coat supplement containing biotin, zinc, and essential fatty acids. These combination products address multiple pathways to healthy skin and coat.

Supplement Dosage Guide

Getting dosages right matters. Too little won't help; too much can cause problems.

Fish Oil (for small dogs)

General guideline: 300mg combined EPA/DHA per 10 pounds body weight weekly. For a 10-pound dog: 300mg per week, or about 43mg daily. For a 5-pound dog: 150mg weekly, or about 21mg daily.

From whole fish: 2-3 sardines (about 20g each) provide roughly 200-400mg omega-3s. One small salmon fillet (about 50g) might provide 500-1000mg. Adjust whole fish feeding based on the omega-3 content of specific species.

Vitamin E

2-3 IU per pound body weight daily. For a 10-pound dog: 20-30 IU daily. Most fish oil capsules contain vitamin E already—check labels. One 1000IU capsule divided among multiple doses provides weeks of supplementation for small dogs.

CoQ10 (for cardiac support)

30-100mg daily for small dogs. Use the reduced form (ubiquinol) for better absorption. Higher doses may be appropriate for dogs with existing heart conditions, but discuss with your veterinarian first.

Probiotics

If using commercial products, follow manufacturer guidelines for dogs. For fermented foods, start with 1/2 teaspoon for small dogs and increase to 1-2 teaspoons if tolerated.

Supplements to Skip

Not all supplements are worth your money. Some are essentially useless; others can cause harm.

Glucosamine and Chondroitin (for most dogs)

These popular joint supplements have weak evidence for efficacy in dogs. The research supporting them is mixed at best. Green-lipped mussel provides natural glucosamine and chondroitin along with omega-3s—it's a better choice if you want joint support. For most raw-fed dogs eating complete diets, joint supplements aren't necessary.

Multivitamins

Raw diets are complete. Adding multivitamins risks creating imbalances, especially fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) that accumulate in body tissues. Too much vitamin A from supplements can cause toxicity. Unless your vet specifically recommends a multivitamin, skip them.

Calcium Without Bone Content

If you're feeding appropriate bone content (10% of the diet), you don't need calcium supplements. Adding extra calcium without balancing with other minerals can cause problems. Bone meal or ground eggshells might be appropriate if bone content is consistently low, but they're rarely needed.

Protein Powders

Raw diets are already protein-rich. Adding protein supplements wastes money and can unbalance the calcium-to-phosphorus ratio. Your dog doesn't need protein powder unless you're severely underfeeding muscle meat.

Getting Nutrients Through Food First

Food should always be your first source of nutrition. Supplements fill gaps; they don't replace real food.

Omega-3s from Fish

Whole fish is often better than fish oil supplements. Sardines, mackerel, salmon, and herring provide omega-3s along with protein, vitamin D, selenium, and other nutrients. Feed fish 1-2 times weekly. Canned sardines (in water, no salt added) work well.

Vitamin E from Food

Organ meats, especially liver, provide vitamin E. Most raw diets contain adequate vitamin E from natural food sources. Adding supplemental vitamin E is mainly necessary when feeding polyunsaturated fats like fish oil.

Probiotics from Fermented Foods

Plain kefir and fermented vegetables provide probiotics in natural forms. These are often better absorbed than commercial products and come with additional nutrients.

Safe Supplement Stacking

Most supplements work well together. Here's how to combine them safely.

Fish oil and vitamin E are companions—always give vitamin E when supplementing omega-3s. Give fish oil with meals for better absorption.

Probiotics can be given alongside any other supplement. Some people give them first thing in the morning; others mix them into food.

Space out supplements that might compete for absorption. Calcium can interfere with zinc absorption if given simultaneously. If your dog takes both, separate doses by a few hours.

When in doubt about combining supplements, simplify. The raw diet provides most of what your dog needs. Supplements address specific concerns—don't create a supplement regimen more complex than necessary.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do small dogs on raw diets really need supplements?

A varied, properly formulated raw diet provides most nutrients dogs need. However, fish oil (omega-3s) benefits nearly all dogs for anti-inflammatory support. Other supplements address specific breed concerns or fill gaps in food quality. For most dogs, one or two targeted supplements are adequate—not a cabinet full of pills.

How much fish oil should I give my small dog?

About 300mg combined EPA/DHA per 10 pounds of body weight weekly. This can come from whole fish (2-3 sardines per week for a 10-pound dog) or from supplements. If feeding sardines, you may not need additional fish oil at all.

Can I give human supplements to my dog?

Many human supplements are fine for dogs in appropriate doses. Fish oil, vitamin E, and CoQ10 are commonly used in both human and veterinary medicine. Avoid supplements with xylitol (common in gummy vitamins) or other harmful additives. Always check with your vet if unsure.

What supplements help small breeds with heart concerns?

CoQ10, omega-3s from fish oil, and taurine support cardiac health. These supplements won't prevent genetic heart disease but may slow progression and support overall heart function. Discuss supplementation with your veterinarian, especially if your dog is on heart medications.

My vet says supplements are useless. Should I listen?

Many vets receive minimal nutrition education and rely on commercial pet food marketing. That said, vets are right to be skeptical of unnecessary supplements. If your raw diet is complete and varied, you may not need much supplementation. But targeted use of quality supplements for specific concerns can provide genuine benefits.

Are green-lipped mussel supplements better than glucosamine?

Yes, green-lipped mussel provides natural glucosamine and chondroitin along with omega-3s and other compounds. Research supports green-lipped mussel for joint health in dogs. It's a better choice than isolated glucosamine/chondroitin supplements, though most raw-fed dogs don't need joint supplements at all.

Can supplements replace a complete raw diet?

No. Supplements complement a complete raw diet—they don't replace real food. A raw diet provides protein, fat, vitamins, minerals, and other nutrients in bioavailable forms that supplements cannot replicate. Use supplements to address specific gaps or concerns, not as shortcuts to proper nutrition.
