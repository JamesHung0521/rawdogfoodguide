Cavalier King Charles Spaniel Raw Diet: Heart-Healthy Feeding

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels are elegant, affectionate companions with silky coats and soulful eyes that seem to understand you completely. These beloved dogs deserve nutrition that honors their royal heritage and protects their vulnerable hearts. If you're feeding a Cavalier, understanding the breed's specific health risks will help you make choices that extend and improve their lives.

Understanding Cavalier Nutrition

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels typically weigh between 12 and 18 pounds, with a graceful, medium-boned structure. Despite their lap-dog appearance, they're active little spaniels who enjoy walks, play, and even brief hunting antics. They need quality nutrition to support their moderate activity level and maintain the coat and skin health they're famous for.

The breed's history matters for understanding their nutritional needs. They were bred as lap dogs for British nobility, but they retain working spaniel instincts and anatomy. This means they're more robust and active than their cuddly appearance suggests. Their long, floppy ears served historically to protect hearing during hunting, and their sweet expression masks a surprisingly athletic dog.

Cavaliers are prone to several serious health conditions that good nutrition can influence: mitral valve disease (MVD), which affects nearly every Cavalier by age 10; syringomyelia, a neurological condition causing spinal cord damage; and various joint issues. While genetics play the primary role in these conditions, inflammation, oxidative stress, and body weight all modify how and when conditions develop.

Raw feeding supports Cavaliers because it reduces inflammatory ingredients common in processed foods. Many owners notice improved coat quality, better energy levels, and easier weight management when switching from kibble. The omega-3 fatty acids in raw meat and fish provide natural anti-inflammatory benefits that may slow degenerative conditions. For an introduction to raw feeding basics, see our beginner's guide.

Heart Health & MVD

Mitral Valve Disease deserves special attention for every Cavalier owner. MVD affects the mitral valve on the left side of the heart, causing blood to leak backward through the valve. This progressive condition eventually leads to heart failure. By age five, approximately 50% of Cavaliers show signs of MVD. By age 10, that number climbs to nearly 100%.

While MVD is primarily genetic, nutrition can influence how quickly the condition progresses. The goal is to support overall cardiovascular health, reduce inflammation in blood vessels, and maintain healthy body weight to minimize cardiac workload.

Taurine is one of the most important amino acids for heart health. Dogs can synthesize taurine, but some individuals—especially those with certain genetic makeups—may benefit from dietary sources. Raw red meat contains taurine naturally. Beef heart is particularly rich in this amino acid, making it an excellent addition to a Cavalier's diet.

Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10) supports energy production in heart cells. Some studies suggest CoQ10 supplementation may slow the progression of heart disease in dogs. While research is ongoing, many holistic veterinarians recommend it for breeds prone to cardiac issues. Natural food sources include organ meats, particularly heart, though supplementation may be beneficial for Cavaliers with existing heart concerns.

Omega-3 fatty acids from fish oil reduce inflammation in blood vessels and may help maintain normal heart rhythm. The EPA and DHA in fish oil provide cardiovascular benefits without the inflammatory omega-6 fatty acids found in many plant oils. Salmon, sardines, and mackerel are excellent sources. Aim for 1-2 servings of fatty fish per week as part of a varied raw diet rotation.

Weight Management

Maintaining ideal weight is crucial for Cavaliers with heart concerns. Every extra pound puts additional strain on a heart that's already working harder than it should. A lean body condition reduces cardiac workload and can significantly extend both the quality and length of a Cavalier's life.

Cavaliers should have a visible waist when viewed from above and you should be able to feel their ribs without pressing hard. A tucked abdomen when viewed from the side also indicates healthy weight. If you can't see or feel ribs easily, your Cavalier is carrying excess weight.

Raw feeding makes weight management easier because protein and fat satisfy hunger more effectively than carbohydrates. Dogs on raw diets typically eat less by volume while feeling equally satisfied. The high moisture content of raw food also contributes to feelings of fullness.

Portion sizes for adult Cavaliers typically range from 2-2.5% of body weight daily. A 15-pound Cavalier needs approximately 150-190 grams (5-6.5 ounces) of raw food per day, split into two meals. Adjust based on your individual dog's metabolism, activity level, and body condition. Weight should be checked monthly once you've established appropriate portions.

If your Cavalier needs to lose weight, reduce portions by 10-15% while monitoring energy levels and appetite. Weight loss should be gradual—no more than 1-2% of body weight per week. Rapid weight loss strains the liver and can be dangerous. If your Cavalier seems constantly hungry despite appropriate portions, increase the bone content slightly (bone provides satiety) or add more fibrous organ content.

Omega-3 & CoQ10 Supplementation

While a well-constructed raw diet provides most nutrients naturally, Cavaliers often benefit from targeted supplementation to support their heart health.

Fish oil is the foundation of supplementation for Cavaliers. Look for products specifically formulated for pets, or use human-grade fish oil without added flavors or sweeteners. The standard recommendation is approximately 300mg of combined EPA/DHA per 10 pounds of body weight daily. This can come from whole fish meals or supplemental oil. Salmon oil is particularly popular due to its balanced fatty acid profile.

Vitamin E works synergistically with omega-3s and should be provided when feeding polyunsaturated fats. The selenium in raw meat provides antioxidant protection, but some dogs may benefit from additional vitamin E. The typical dose is 2-3 IU per pound of body weight, though consult your vet for specific recommendations.

CoQ10 supplementation ranges from 30-100mg daily for small dogs, depending on whether your Cavalier has existing heart issues. Ubiquinol (the reduced form) is better absorbed than ubiquinone. Many owners start supplementation around age 5-6 as a preventive measure, or earlier if MVD has been diagnosed. Discuss timing and dosage with your veterinarian.

Krill oil is an alternative to fish oil that contains phospholipid-bound omega-3s, which may be more bioavailable. It also contains astaxanthin, a powerful antioxidant. Some owners prefer krill for these reasons, though it's more expensive than fish oil. Either source provides adequate omega-3s when dosed properly.

Hawthorn berry is an herbal supplement sometimes recommended for dogs with heart concerns. It may help strengthen heart contractions and has mild diuretic properties. However, it should only be used under veterinary guidance, especially if your Cavalier is on heart medications, due to potential interactions.

Daily Portion Guide

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels typically range from 12-18 pounds as adults. Use your individual dog's weight for calculations, not breed averages.

Adult Cavaliers generally need 2-2.5% of body weight daily. A 14-pound adult might need 140-175 grams (about 5-6 ounces) per day, divided into two meals. Highly active Cavaliers may need closer to 3%, while seniors or less active dogs often do well on 1.5-2%.

Puppies have dramatically different needs. Cavalier puppies need 5-8% of body weight daily, split across three or four meals. Their developing hearts and bodies need substantial nutrition. Underfeeding puppies can cause hypoglycemia, weakness, and failure to thrive. Never restrict a Cavalier puppy's food access without veterinary guidance.

Pregnant and nursing Cavaliers have increased nutritional needs. During pregnancy, gradually increase food to 125-150% of normal intake. Lactating mothers may need 200-300% of their normal portions, fed in multiple small meals throughout the day. The physical demands of nursing are enormous—mother dogs can lose significant body condition without adequate nutrition.

For Cavaliers with early-stage MVD, maintaining consistent, appropriately sized portions supports heart function. Avoid high-sodium foods (some raw diets can be higher in sodium than expected, particularly from pork or fish) if your vet has recommended sodium restriction. Monitor for symptoms of fluid retention, such as increased panting or decreased appetite.

Protein Selection

Cavaliers generally thrive on diverse protein sources, but certain proteins offer specific benefits for this breed's needs.

Beef, particularly beef heart, deserves emphasis for Cavaliers. Heart muscle is rich in taurine, CoQ10, and other compounds that support cardiac function. Including beef heart 2-3 times per week provides these nutrients in their most bioavailable form. Many Cavalier owners make heart a staple rather than an occasional treat.

Fatty fish supports heart health through omega-3s. Salmon, sardines, mackerel, and herring are all excellent choices. Feed fish 1-2 times per week. Raw fish from reputable sources is generally safe; freezing for 24 hours provides additional parasite protection. Sardines (whole, with bones) are one of the most nutritious and affordable fish options.

Turkey and chicken provide lean protein and are usually well-digested. Dark meat from these birds contains more zinc and iron than white meat. Chicken thighs with bone provide calcium in a highly digestible form. For Cavaliers who need to manage weight, breast meat is leaner, though less flavorful.

Rabbit is an excellent novel protein for Cavaliers who may have developed sensitivities to more common proteins. It's lean, highly digestible, and many dogs find it palatable. Rabbit also tends to be raised without antibiotics and growth hormones, which appeals to owners focused on clean nutrition.

Organ content should follow the standard 10% rule: 5% liver, 5% other secreting organs. For Cavaliers, prioritize heart as an organ source when possible. Kidney, spleen, and brain provide different nutrient profiles that complement muscle meat in a complete diet.

Senior Cavalier Considerations

Cavaliers are considered seniors around age 7-8, though many show impressive vitality well beyond this. Senior Cavaliers often need adjusted nutrition to account for changing metabolism, decreased activity, and progression of breed-specific health conditions.

Digestion may become less efficient with age. Smaller, more frequent meals (three rather than two) can help seniors absorb nutrients better. Some older Cavaliers benefit from slightly softer food textures, particularly if dental issues have developed.

Kidney function should be monitored in senior Cavaliers. While commercial renal diets are designed for kidney disease, a raw diet can be adjusted to support kidney health with proper guidance. Lower phosphorus content may be appropriate for dogs with diagnosed kidney issues. Always work with your vet for senior dogs with health conditions.

Joint health becomes increasingly important as Cavaliers age. The omega-3s in raw fish continue to support joint comfort. Green-lipped mussel is a supplement some owners add for joint support—it contains glucosamine, chondroitin, and omega-3s in a natural form.

Mental stimulation through feeding can benefit senior Cavaliers. Puzzle feeders, food-dispensing toys, and hand-feeding sessions maintain the bond between owner and dog while encouraging gentle activity and engagement. This is especially valuable for seniors who may be less active physically.

Frequently Asked Questions

What supplements should I give my Cavalier King Charles on a raw diet?

Fish oil (omega-3s) is the most important supplement for Cavaliers. Consider adding CoQ10, especially after age 5. Vitamin E provides antioxidant support when feeding polyunsaturated fats. A varied raw diet with proper organ content should provide everything else. Consult your veterinarian for specific recommendations based on your dog's health status and any diagnosed conditions.

How does raw feeding help Cavaliers with MVD?

Raw feeding reduces inflammatory ingredients that contribute to cardiovascular inflammation. The omega-3s in fish reduce blood vessel inflammation and may support normal heart rhythm. Maintaining lean body condition reduces cardiac workload. Taurine from red meat and heart supports heart muscle function. While raw feeding won't prevent MVD, it supports overall cardiovascular health.

Can I feed my Cavalier beef heart?

Yes, beef heart is excellent for Cavaliers. It's a muscle organ rich in taurine and CoQ10, both crucial for heart health. Feed beef heart as part of the 10% organ content (it counts as muscle meat, not a secreting organ). Many Cavalier owners feed heart 2-3 times weekly. It can be fed raw, cut into appropriate-sized pieces for your dog.

How do I know if my Cavalier is at a healthy weight?

Feel for ribs along the chest—you should be able to count them without pressing. View from above: there should be a visible waist that tucks in behind the ribs. From the side, the abdomen should tuck upward. No visible fat deposits, and you should see a slight hourglass shape. If ribs are hidden under fat, reduce portions. If ribs are prominently visible, increase portions.

Should senior Cavaliers eat differently than younger ones?

Seniors often need fewer calories due to decreased metabolism, but protein needs remain high to maintain muscle mass. Consider adding more omega-3s for joint and cognitive support. Feed smaller, more frequent meals if digestion seems impaired. Joint supplements may become beneficial. The core diet—raw meat, bone, and organs—remains appropriate for most seniors.

How often should I feed my Cavalier?

Adult Cavaliers do well with two meals daily. Puppies need 3-4 meals due to hypoglycemia risk and growth demands. Seniors may benefit from three smaller meals. Consistent feeding times support healthy digestion. Avoid feeding immediately before or after vigorous exercise, especially for Cavaliers with heart conditions.

What protein sources are best for Cavaliers with sensitive stomachs?

Start with chicken or turkey if your Cavalier has digestive sensitivities. These proteins are usually well-tolerated. Introduce new proteins one at a time, waiting 5-7 days between rotations. If sensitivities persist, try rabbit or venison as novel proteins. Avoid high-fat proteins initially, and keep organ content conservative until digestion stabilizes.
