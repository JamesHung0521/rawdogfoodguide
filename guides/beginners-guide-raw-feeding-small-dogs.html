Beginner's Guide to Raw Feeding for Small Dogs

Making the switch to raw food can feel overwhelming at first. There's a lot of information out there, and sometimes it seems like everyone has a different opinion on what's "right." If you've been considering raw feeding for your small dog but don't know where to start, you're in the right place. This guide walks you through everything a beginner needs to know.

What Is Raw Feeding?

Raw feeding means giving your dog a diet that mimics what their ancestors ate in the wild. Instead of kibble or canned food, you serve uncooked meat, bones, organs, and sometimes vegetables. The idea is that dogs evolved to digest these foods, and many pet owners report improvements in coat quality, energy levels, dental health, and digestion after making the switch.

The raw diet movement gained real momentum in the 1990s, though people have been feeding scraps and table scraps to working dogs for centuries. What started as a response to the rise of heavily processed commercial pet foods has grown into a passionate community of home feeders who believe in getting back to basics.

For small dogs specifically, raw feeding offers some unique advantages. Their fast metabolisms mean they often digest raw meat more easily than large breeds. Plus, the dental benefits matter a lot for toy and small breeds who are prone to dental disease. But small dogs also come with their own set of challenges—smaller bodies have less room for error when it comes to portion sizes, and some breeds have specific health concerns you need to keep in mind.

Why Choose Raw for Small Dogs?

Small dogs aren't just scaled-down versions of larger breeds. They have distinct nutritional needs and physiological differences that make diet choices especially important.

One of the biggest draws is dental health. Small breed dogs spend a lifetime fighting dental disease. Kibble, despite marketing claims, doesn't actually clean teeth—the starches in most dry foods stick to teeth and contribute to plaque buildup. Raw meaty bones provide natural teeth cleaning through chewing, and many small dog owners notice fresher breath and cleaner teeth within weeks of switching.

Energy levels often improve dramatically. When your dog is burning glucose from carbs all day, they experience energy spikes and crashes. A protein and fat-based diet provides steady, sustained energy. Small dogs especially seem to benefit from this—they're often described as having "busy" temperaments, and proper nutrition gives them fuel for their antics without the crash.

Skin and coat health shows visible improvements too. Many small breeds, particularly those with long coats like Yorkies and Maltese, struggle with dry skin, itching, and coat quality issues. The bioavailable nutrients in raw food—omega fatty acids from fresh meat, zinc from organ meats, and vitamins from whole food sources—are absorbed more efficiently than synthetic supplements added to processed foods.

Small dogs also tend to have more sensitive digestive systems. Processed ingredients in commercial foods can cause inflammation and irritation. Raw food is easier to digest because it contains natural enzymes that break down during cooking. This means less gas, smaller stool volume (raw diets produce significantly less waste), and more predictable digestion.

BARF vs. Prey Model: What's the Difference?

If you've been researching raw feeding, you've probably encountered these two terms. Both approaches aim to replicate a natural canine diet, but they go about it differently.

The Prey Model focuses on replicating an entire animal. The typical ratio is 80% muscle meat, 10% bone, 5% liver, and 5% other secreting organs (kidney, spleen, brain, etc.). Followers of this model argue that dogs in the wild would eat herbivores whole—prey that provides exactly these proportions. There's no plant matter involved in the classic Prey Model formulation.

The BARF Diet (Biologically Appropriate Raw Food, or Bones and Raw Food) includes some plant matter—typically 10-20% vegetables and sometimes small amounts of fruit. The theory is that wild dogs and wolves get some plant material from the digestive tracts of their herbivore prey. BARF proponents believe this adds fiber, antioxidants, and phytonutrients that benefit overall health.

For small dogs, either approach can work well. Some small breeds do better with slightly more bone content (the extra crunching helps clean tiny teeth), while others with sensitive digestion may prefer the simpler Prey Model. Many experienced feeders land somewhere in the middle, using BARF principles as general guidance while adjusting based on how their individual dog thrives.

Special Considerations for Small Dogs

Before you start chopping up chicken thighs, there are some important things to keep in mind about small breed physiology.

Bone size matters. A Great Dane can handle a chicken neck without much thought. Your five-pound Chihuahua cannot. Small dogs need appropriately sized bones—finely ground bone is often safer for toy breeds, while small breed dogs can usually manage cut-up raw chicken wings or small quail.

Low blood sugar is a real risk. Small dogs have high surface-area-to-volume ratios, which means they lose heat and energy faster. Toy breeds especially are prone to hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), which can become dangerous if they skip meals or get over-excited. Always ensure your small dog eats regularly, and keep a glucose supplement handy for emergencies.

Body weight calculations are more critical. With a large dog, being off by a quarter-pound on portion size barely registers. With a five-pound Yorkie, that same quarter-pound represents a significant miscalculation. Small dogs need more precise measuring to maintain healthy weight.

Dental fractures are a concern. While chewing bones is great for dental health, small dogs have small, delicate teeth. Bones that are too hard can cause fractures. Always supervise bone chewing, and consider softer raw meaty bones from younger animals rather than dense weight-bearing bones from adults.

How to Get Started

The transition to raw food doesn't have to happen overnight. In fact, a gradual switch often works better for dogs with sensitive stomachs.

Week One: Preparation

Before you buy any meat, research your local sources. Grocery store meat works fine for beginners—it's inspected, safe, and readily available. You'll need to invest in a good cutting board, sharp knives, and freezer space for bulk purchases. Consider what proteins are easily accessible and affordable in your area.

Week Two: Introduction

Start by replacing one meal per day with raw food. Offer a simple meal of muscle meat mixed with a little organ meat (about 5% of the meal). Watch for any digestive upset, changes in stool quality, or behavioral changes. Some dogs take to raw immediately; others need a few days to adjust.

Week Three: Full Transition

Assuming week two goes smoothly, you can start replacing more meals. Many small dogs do well with two meals per day rather than one—again, the hypoglycemia risk is real, and smaller, more frequent meals support stable energy levels.

Week Four: Fine-Tuning

This is when you adjust ratios based on your dog's condition. Are they losing too much weight? Add more fat. Gaining too much? Reduce portions slightly. Stools too soft? You might be using too much organ meat or need more bone content.

Understanding Portion Sizes

Getting portions right is probably the most intimidating part of raw feeding for beginners. The good news is that it's more forgiving than you might think.

The general starting point is 2-3% of your dog's body weight in raw food per day for adult dogs. An active dog might need closer to 3%, while a less active or older dog might do well on 2%. For small dogs, this calculation becomes especially important—our Raw Feeding Portion Calculator helps you figure out exact amounts based on breed, weight, and activity level.

Puppies have different needs. Growing small breed puppies often need 5-10% of their body weight daily, split across three or four meals. Their developing bodies are building bone and muscle rapidly, and they simply can't take in enough calories in two meals to support healthy growth.

Weight should be monitored weekly for the first month, then monthly thereafter. Adjust portions based on what you see—ribs should be felt but not seen, and your dog should have a visible waist when viewed from above. Breeding dogs, active dogs, and senior dogs all need individualized adjustments from baseline calculations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even well-intentioned feeders run into problems. Here's how to sidestep the most common pitfalls.

Skipping the bone content. Some people get squeamish about feeding bones, but raw bones are safe when appropriately sized. Bone provides calcium and phosphorus, essential minerals that are harder to balance otherwise. Without bone, you risk nutritional deficiencies and poor dental outcomes.

Too much organ meat. Organ meats are nutrient powerhouses, but more isn't better. Liver, especially, can cause vitamin A toxicity if overfed. Stick to the 5% rule for liver and other secreting organs, and your dog will get all the benefits without the risks.

Ignoring variety. Dogs thrive on variety. Feeding the same protein source day after day limits the nutrient profile and can lead to sensitivities. Rotate between chicken, beef, turkey, lamb, pork, and fish over time. Each protein source offers different amino acid profiles and micronutrients.

Not transitioning gradually. Switching abruptly from kibble to raw can cause digestive upset. The bacteria populations in your dog's gut need time to adapt. A gradual transition also lets you spot any adverse reactions before they've become a pattern.

Forgetting about food safety. Raw feeding means handling raw meat. Wash your hands thoroughly, clean surfaces with pet-safe disinfectants, and store food properly. These precautions protect both you and your dog. Our Food Safety Guide covers everything you need to know about handling and storage.

Comparing to other dogs. Your neighbor's Border Collie might thrive on one approach, but your Dachshund is a different animal entirely. Breed, age, activity level, metabolism, and individual health all influence what works best. Use other people's experiences as starting points, not formulas.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to see results from raw feeding?

Most owners notice improvements in energy and stool quality within the first two weeks. Coat and skin improvements typically show up within 4-8 weeks. Dental improvements accumulate over months and years of consistent bone chewing. Every dog is different, so give it at least 8-12 weeks before deciding whether raw feeding is working for your pet.

Can I mix raw food with kibble?

Technically yes, but it's not ideal. Dogs produce different digestive enzymes for different food types, and mixing can lead to incomplete digestion of both. If you must transition gradually, some dogs do fine with one raw meal and one kibble meal per day, but complete raw feeding generally produces better results.

What if my dog refuses to eat raw food?

Start with something irresistible—many dogs who turn up their noses at chicken thighs go wild for chicken livers. You can also try warming the food slightly (not cooked, just to body temperature), adding a splash of bone broth, or hand feeding to build positive associations. Hunger eventually wins, but never starve a dog into eating.

Do small dogs really need to eat bones?

Yes, bone is an essential part of a complete raw diet. It provides calcium and phosphorus in the correct ratios, plus the mechanical chewing action cleans teeth and exercises jaw muscles. For very small dogs or those with dental concerns, ground bone or softer bones from young animals work well. Check out our breed-specific guides like Chihuahua and Yorkshire Terrier feeding guides for size-appropriate recommendations.

How do I handle raw food safely with children in the house?

Keep raw food prep separate from areas where children's food is handled. Use dedicated cutting boards and utensils for raw meat. Wash hands thoroughly after handling. Store raw food on the lowest shelf of the refrigerator or in the freezer—never above ready-to-eat foods. Children old enough to understand should learn that dog food is for dogs only, not for touching or tasting.

Is raw feeding expensive?

Costs vary significantly by location and protein source. Chicken-based diets are usually the most affordable. Buying in bulk from wholesale clubs or directly from farms reduces costs. Many owners find that raw feeding costs roughly comparable to premium kibble when purchased strategically. The health benefits often translate to fewer vet bills over time, offsetting some of the food costs.

My vet seems opposed to raw feeding. What should I do?

Not all vets are familiar with or supportive of raw feeding, though this is changing. Look for a vet who takes a integrative or functional medicine approach, or one who specializes in nutrition. Many raw feeders develop productive relationships with skeptical vets by sharing peer-reviewed research and documenting their dog's health outcomes. Your vet's concerns often stem from lack of information rather than evidence against raw diets.
