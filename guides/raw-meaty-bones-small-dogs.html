Raw Meaty Bones for Small Dogs: Safe Choices & Feeding Guide

Raw bones are non-negotiable in a complete raw diet. They provide calcium and phosphorus in ideal ratios, support dental health, exercise jaw muscles, and satisfy your dog's natural chewing instincts. But small dogs need appropriately sized bones, and some bones are simply dangerous regardless of size. This guide covers everything you need to know about safely incorporating raw meaty bones into your small dog's diet.

Why Bones Matter in Raw Diets

Bones aren't just an optional add-on to a raw diet—they're an essential component. A complete raw meal without bone is missing crucial nutrients and the mechanical chewing action that keeps teeth clean and jaws strong.

The calcium in raw bones is highly bioavailable. Unlike calcium supplements that must compete for absorption with other minerals, bone calcium comes prepackaged with phosphorus in roughly the 1.5:1 to 2:1 ratio that dogs absorb best. This natural balance is difficult to replicate with supplements or ground eggshells.

Beyond nutrition, bones provide behavioral benefits. Chewing occupies dogs mentally and physically. Small dogs who don't get to crunch and gnaw often develop behavioral issues, anxiety, or destructive tendencies. A bone satisfies the chewing instinct that kibble feeding eliminates entirely.

Dental health might be the most compelling reason to feed bones. Small breed dogs are disproportionately affected by dental disease. The mechanical action of gnawing on raw bones scrapes away plaque before it hardens into calculus. Regular bone chewing can reduce or eliminate the need for professional dental cleanings, which require anesthesia and carry their own risks.

Raw bones are safe when fed properly. The "dangerous bones" concern applies to cooked bones, which become brittle and can splinter. Raw bones remain soft enough to chew safely. The risks come from improper sizing, unsupervised feeding, or feeding the wrong types of bones. With knowledge and supervision, bone feeding is remarkably safe.

Safe Bones for Small Dogs

These bones are appropriate for small dogs when sized correctly and fed under supervision.

Chicken Necks

Chicken necks are one of the best bones for small dogs. They're appropriately sized for tiny mouths, highly palatable, and provide excellent bone content. The cartilage and connective tissue add nutritional value and make them easier to chew than bony segments alone.

For very small dogs (under 5 pounds), cut chicken necks into smaller pieces. For dogs 5-15 pounds, whole chicken necks work well. Larger small dogs (15-25 pounds) can handle slightly larger portions. Chicken necks should be fed raw, never cooked.

Chicken Wings

Chicken wings provide more meat relative to bone than necks. They're good for small dogs who need to gain weight or who seem less interested in bony cuts. The wing tips contain soft bone surrounded by meat, making them manageable for most small dogs.

For small dogs, chicken wingettes (the middle section without the tip) or small whole wings work well. Cut into segments if needed for very small dogs. Some owners prefer to leave wings whole for the gnawing experience they provide.

Quail

Whole quail is an excellent bone source for small dogs. Quail bones are smaller and softer than chicken bones, making them ideal for toy breeds and small dogs with delicate jaws. A whole quail provides complete nutrition in an appropriate package.

Quail can be fed whole (if sized appropriately) or cut into portions. Many small dogs love the variety of whole prey-style feeding. Quail is more expensive than chicken but provides excellent nutrition and bone content.

Turkey Necks

Turkey necks are larger than chicken necks but still manageable for medium-sized small dogs (15+ pounds). They provide more bone content and require more chewing effort, which benefits dental health.

Cut turkey necks into appropriate portions for your dog's size. Smaller segments work better than long pieces. The bone in turkey necks is slightly harder than chicken, so ensure your dog can manage it before offering larger pieces.

Rabbit

Whole rabbit (small enough for your dog to consume entirely) provides complete nutrition with appropriate bone content. Rabbit bones are small and soft. Many small dogs thrive on whole prey-style feeding with appropriately sized rabbits.

Commercially available whole prey items are typically sorted by size. Choose rabbits that your dog can consume within a single feeding session. For very small dogs, day-old chicks or small game birds might be appropriate alternatives.

Bones to Always Avoid

Some bones should never be fed to any dog, regardless of size or chewing ability.

Cooked Bones of Any Kind

Cooking makes bones brittle and sharp. When chewed, cooked bones splinter into shards that can puncture the digestive tract, lodge in the throat, or cause internal damage. This applies to all cooked bones—boiled, baked, roasted, grilled, or any other preparation. Never feed cooked bones.

Large Weight-Bearing Bones

Beef leg bones, femurs, and knuckle bones are far too hard for small dogs. These bones from adult animals are dense and can fracture small teeth. Even large dogs should avoid these bones; small dogs absolutely must. The risk of broken teeth is high, and tooth fractures require expensive veterinary dental work.

Small Hard Bones from Large Animals

Pork rib bones, ham bones, and similar items from large animals are too dense for small dogs. Even if sized appropriately, these bones can break teeth. The density comes from the animal's age and the bone's function—weight-bearing bones from adult animals are simply too hard.

Fish Bones (Whole)

Small fish bones can lodge in throats or pierce digestive tracts. Some dogs handle raw fish fine (the bones are soft in very fresh fish), but feeding whole fish with bones is generally risky. Feed deboned fish or small fish that will be consumed entirely, or use ground fish instead.

Sharp Bones from Game Animals

Horn cores and antlers are dangerous regardless of the marketing. These are extremely hard and designed to be sharp. Even if your dog doesn't break a tooth, they can gum themselves on these items. Some dogs have been severely injured by antler fragments.

Proper Bone Sizing

Getting the right size matters for safety and benefit.

The "Larger Than Mouth" Rule

For most small dogs, bones should be larger than what they can swallow whole. A bone your dog must chew rather than gulp is safest. This doesn't mean bones should be enormous—it means choosing bones that require active chewing.

Matching to Your Dog

Toy breeds (under 6 pounds): Use chicken neck pieces, small quail, or day-old chicks. Cut larger bones into appropriate portions.

Small breeds (6-15 pounds): Chicken necks, small quail, or chicken wings work well. Some can manage small turkey neck segments.

Small-medium breeds (15-25 pounds): Turkey necks, larger quail, or whole rabbits sized appropriately. Chicken is still appropriate if your dog prefers it.

When in Doubt, Go Smaller

It's better to feed a bone slightly too small than one your dog struggles with. You can always add more bone content through multiple small pieces or ground bone added to meals. A struggling dog might gulp dangerously or damage teeth on oversized bones.

Ground Bone as Alternative

For very small dogs, toy breeds, dogs with dental issues, or simply as a supplement to whole bone feeding, ground bone is an excellent alternative. Purchase bone ground to a fine powder, or grind fresh bones at home with a meat grinder. Add approximately 10% ground bone to muscle meat for calcium content.

How Often to Feed Bones

Bone feeding frequency depends on your goals and your dog's individual needs.

Daily Bone Feeding

Some raw feeders include bone at every meal. This is fine if portions are appropriate and stools remain firm. Daily bone feeding provides consistent calcium intake and continuous dental benefits. For small dogs who enjoy chewing, daily bone meals become a highlight of their day.

2-3 Times Weekly

Many owners feed bone-in meals 2-3 times weekly and serve boneless meals at other times. This rotation provides adequate calcium and regular dental benefits while offering variety. It's easier to manage bone content when you know bone is included on specific days.

Weekly Bone Meal

Even once-weekly bone meals provide significant benefits. Dental health improves with any regular bone chewing. Calcium needs are met over the week even if concentrated into one or two meals. This approach works well for busy owners or dogs who do better with less frequent bone exposure.

Adjusting Based on Stools

Stool quality tells you if bone content is appropriate. Firm, small stools indicate good bone content. White, chalky, crumbly stools suggest too much bone—reduce bone content. Loose stools suggest too little bone—increase bone content. Adjust frequency and portion sizes based on what you observe.

Safe Monitoring Practices

Supervision is essential whenever your small dog eats bones.

Always Supervise

Never leave your dog unattended with bones. Watch for signs of distress, gulping, or difficulty. If something seems wrong, remove the bone and assess. Most accidents happen during unsupervised bone chewing.

Remove Small Pieces

As your dog chews, bones break into smaller pieces. Remove pieces small enough to swallow whole before that happens. If you see your dog trying to gulp a bone fragment, take it away. It's better to lose some bone content than risk a choking incident.

Know the Signs of Trouble

Pawing at the mouth, excessive drooling, gagging without producing anything, or visible distress while chewing all suggest problems. Remove the bone immediately. If your dog is actually choking (not just gagging), you'll see them unable to breathe or make sounds. Learn canine Heimlich maneuver before you need it.

Post-Chew Bone Removal

Once your dog has chewed a bone to small pieces, remove what remains. Don't leave chewed bones lying around. The remaining pieces are more likely to be swallowed inappropriately. Dispose of bones in a secure trash container—some dogs will counter-surf for discarded bones.

Dental Health Benefits

Raw bones provide unmatched dental benefits for small dogs.

Mechanical Plaque Removal

Chewing raw bone scrapes away plaque before it hardens into calculus. The mechanical action reaches areas that brushing misses. Regular bone chewers often have noticeably cleaner teeth and fresher breath compared to dogs on soft diets.

Jaw Exercise

Small dogs often have underdeveloped jaw muscles from generations of kibble feeding, which requires minimal chewing. Bones exercise the masseter and temporalis muscles, building strength and proper jaw development. This is especially important for puppies still growing.

Gum Massage

The pressure of chewing massages gums, increasing blood flow and promoting gum health. Healthy gums support healthy teeth. Regular bone chewing contributes to overall oral health beyond just keeping teeth clean.

Reducing Professional Cleanings

Many small dogs require annual professional dental cleanings under anesthesia. These procedures are expensive and carry risks, especially for brachycephalic breeds or dogs with health concerns. Consistent bone feeding can reduce or eliminate the need for professional intervention. Annual vet dental checkups remain important even for bone-fed dogs.

Breed-Specific Dental Concerns

Small breeds with crowded teeth (like many toy breeds) struggle with plaque buildup in spaces that are hard to clean. Bone chewing helps clean these difficult areas. For breeds prone to dental disease, bone feeding is especially valuable. See our breed-specific guides like Chihuahua and Pomeranian feeding guides for more on dental health.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are raw bones safe for small dogs?

Yes, when properly sized and supervised. Raw bones are soft enough to chew safely. Cooked bones are dangerous. Choose appropriately sized bones (chicken necks, quail, small turkey necks), always supervise, and remove pieces small enough to swallow. With these precautions, raw bones are safe and beneficial.

How often should I feed bones to my small dog?

2-3 times weekly is sufficient for most dogs. Daily bone feeding is fine if stools remain firm. Even once weekly provides dental benefits. Adjust based on your dog's stool quality—white, chalky stools mean too much bone; loose stools mean too little.

What bones should I avoid for small dogs?

Never feed cooked bones of any kind. Avoid large weight-bearing bones (beef femurs, knuckle bones) and bones from large adult animals. These are too hard and risk tooth fractures. Also avoid antlers and horn cores, which are marketed as safe but are actually dangerous.

My dog swallows bones whole. What should I do?

Swallowing whole bones is dangerous. Try larger bones that require more chewing. Feed in a muffin tin or on a plate to slow eating. Consider ground bone instead of whole bones if your dog cannot chew appropriately. Supervise closely and remove any pieces your dog might gulp.

Can raw bones cause constipation?

Too much bone causes constipation or hard, white stools. If this happens, reduce bone content temporarily and ensure adequate hydration. Most constipation resolves within a day or two when bone content is adjusted. Adding some muscle meat or organ content can help move things along.

What if my dog broke a tooth on a bone?

Tooth fractures require veterinary dental care. Signs include reluctance to chew, favoring one side of the mouth, visible damage to teeth, or pawing at the mouth. Broken teeth can become infected and are painful. Prevention through appropriate bone sizing is crucial. If a tooth is damaged, see your vet promptly.

Can I feed ground bone instead of whole bones?

Yes, ground bone is an excellent alternative, especially for toy breeds, dogs with dental issues, or as a supplement to whole bone feeding. Add approximately 10% ground bone to muscle meat for calcium content. Purchase ground bone or grind fresh bones at home.
